## The One-Pager

### The music industry is broken by design.

An artist on a major label deal earns roughly 15 cents of every dollar their music generates. The other 85 cents moves through a system built before streaming existed, optimized for the people who built it — not the people who make the music. Five hundred million dollars goes unallocated every year because the infrastructure connecting artists to their money is too fragmented, too patchwork, and too dependent on paperwork filed correctly across a dozen disconnected organizations. Most artists never know what they were owed. Nobody is charged. The money simply disappears.

The fan who was there before everyone else — who told ten people, who showed up every night, who believed before there was anything to believe in — gets nothing. Spotify doesn't know they existed. The label doesn't care. There is no mechanism in the current system that gives early belief any economic value whatsoever.

**SoundSteak exists to fix both of those things at once.**

And then it goes further. While every other label treats the recording as the end product, SoundSteak treats it as the beginning of a living data asset — one that accumulates value through documentation, grows richer every time an artist answers a question about their creative process, and generates revenue streams that no label has ever thought to build. The music industry has always sold sound. SoundSteak is the first platform that also sells meaning. That is not an incremental improvement. That is a different category of company.

---

### What it is

SoundSteak is a fan-owned record label and music investment platform. Artists are signed through a live annual event. Fans invest directly in specific releases. Every dollar the music earns — streams, sync, live, merchandise — flows back through a transparent, automated system that pays every stakeholder their correct percentage, automatically, with no one in the middle making decisions about money that isn't theirs.

The platform handles everything an artist would otherwise have to navigate alone: LLC formation, PRO registration, ISRC and IPI numbers, SoundExchange enrollment, MLC registration, publishing splits, and the legal infrastructure most artists never know exists — and never get.

---

### How it works

Each year, artists apply. Fans decide who gets signed — through $1 pledges and a verified live video. Selected artists perform at the annual event. When the gong rings, two things happen simultaneously: the recording contract is awarded, and the single's ownership opens for investment. Fans and investors buy in at whatever amount they choose, first come first served. An LLC is formed for the release. The year begins.

Twelve months later, at the next annual event, the single drops. Earnings begin immediately — tracked in real time, distributed automatically, visible to every SteakHolder on a live public dashboard. The music stays in the system earning indefinitely. The artist initiated the exit when they're ready, on their terms.

This document is internal. Some terms used here are operational shorthand and should never appear in public-facing copy.

### Cue / Queue

**The Cue** is the individual prompt — the platform initiating a conversation with a specific person to collect a decision, a signature, a preference, a piece of information, or any other intentional input. It is not a notification. It is not a form. It is the platform saying *you're up* in natural language, calibrated to who the person is and what they're involved in.

**The Queue** is the list of Cues waiting for a participant's attention. Same word, same sound — one refers to the individual prompt, one refers to the collection of them.

Cues replace everything that would otherwise require a separate surface: governance decisions, contract signatures, split confirmations, milestone approvals, preference declarations, identity verification, AI licensing choices, publishing split acknowledgments. Every action the platform needs from a participant arrives as a Cue — one consistent interface regardless of what's being collected.

**The Cue scales across every participant type:**

- An artist's Cue might ask them to confirm their AI licensing preference before signing
- A band member's Cue presents the split declaration their bandmate submitted and asks if they agree
- A SteakHolder's Cue asks them to weigh in on a milestone decision — this is a **governance vote**
- A fan's Cue surfaces a preference question about an upcoming tour
- A collaborator's Cue asks them to confirm their credit before it's published

Same interface. Different content. Tailored to exactly who is being asked and why.

**On "governance votes":**

Governance votes are a specific type of Cue — the formal mechanism through which SteakHolders participate in decisions about artist milestones, platform priorities, and release direction. The term should be preserved in SteakHolder-facing language because it accurately describes what's happening (a structured vote with real consequence) and carries the weight that investors expect. Calling it a Cue internally is correct; calling it a governance vote when talking to SteakHolders is also correct. The two terms describe the same event from different vantage points — the platform's plumbing vs. the investor's experience of it.

**Cue Card — the individual prompt unit:**

A Cue Card is a single discrete prompt within the Cue system — the specific question, vote, or action item the platform is presenting to a participant at a given moment. When Cues appear as visual elements in the UI (cards in a stack, items in a queue view, tiles on a dashboard), they are Cue Cards. When the interface is fully conversational, the term still applies conceptually — a Cue Card is the unit of one ask, regardless of the surface it arrives on.

The term earns its place on two levels: the literal (a cue card is what someone holds up to tell you what to say next — exactly what the platform is doing) and the musical (cue + card, from within the same family of terminology). It gives participants a concrete mental image of what they're looking at: not a notification, not a form, not a task — a card. You work through your cards.

Working vocabulary:
- *"You have 3 Cue Cards waiting."*
- *"The governance vote arrived as a Cue Card in your Queue."*
- *"Pull up your Cues — there are two new cards."*

**Why it's called a Cue:**

In music, a cue is the signal that it's your turn to come in. The platform gives you your cue. It knows when you're needed and what for. The word also aligns with queue — the list you work through — so both meanings reinforce each other without needing separate terminology. "Check your cues" means both "look at what the platform needs from you" and "here's where you are in the line."

## The Founding Principle

> **Everyone gets paid their cut of the steak.**

Not just the artist. Not just the investor. Everyone who touches the music — who records it, books it, promotes it, shares it, believes in it early — is documented, protected, and paid automatically through the same transparent system.

This is not a policy. It is an architectural fact about how money moves inside this system.

The music industry's most common abuse doesn't always look like theft. Sometimes it is — a manager skimming before anything passes down, a band leader sitting on the account indefinitely, a label delaying accounting for years while interest accumulates in their favor. But more often it is something quieter and harder to prosecute: **theft by omission**. Money that was earned, that legally belongs to someone, that simply never arrives — not because a villain took it, but because the system is so fragmented, so patchwork, so dependent on paperwork filed correctly across a dozen disconnected organizations, that it falls through the cracks and disappears. No one is charged. No one is held accountable. The engineer just never gets paid their mechanical royalty. The show promoter's percentage never clears. The fan who drove ten people to that first show gets nothing while the platform that hosted the stream earns ad revenue. They often never know what they were owed.

This is not a new problem. It is not a secret. The £500 million that goes unallocated every year is not hidden — Björn Ulvaeus and Max Martin have been speaking publicly about it since 2016. The people who understand it best have spent years trying to raise awareness, build coalitions, and advocate for change through the proper channels.

Nothing has changed.

No aging senator is coming with the political will or technical knowledge to legislate a fix. No lobbying group represents the unregistered bassist who never knew she had an IPI number. The people who profit from the inefficiency — the organizations that earn float on unallocated funds, the intermediaries who collect percentages at every gap — have no incentive to close the gaps they live inside.

Reform from the outside has been tried. It has not worked. It will not work.

**We are not reforming the system. We are replacing it — by building something better from scratch and making it so obviously, demonstrably superior that the industry follows.**

This platform extends that protection beyond the artist and investor to every participant in the ecosystem. The recording engineer. The mixing engineer. The show promoter. The music video director. The photographer. The influencer who posted about the band before anyone knew who they were. The fan who shared the single on the day it dropped and drove the first thousand streams. Every one of them has a profile. Every one of them has a documented role. Every one of them gets paid their cut — automatically, transparently, permanently.

A bassist who gets fired, fairly or unfairly, receives their cut of that recording forever. The band's hostility toward them is irrelevant. The system does not feel it. The same is true for the engineer who mixed the record, the photographer who shot the press kit, the promoter who booked the first show, and the fan who told fifty people to go.

That is the differentiator. Not a better contract. Not a nicer label. Not a reform proposal. A system where everyone who contributes to the success of a piece of music is recognized, documented, and paid — and where the theft by omission that has always been possible is structurally impossible.

Everything else in this platform is built on top of that.

## The Two-Stage Financial Structure

### Stage 1 — Pledge Raise
*Proves the artist has a real audience. Covers onboarding costs only.*

- $1 per pledge
- Target: 300+ pledges ($300+ raised)
- Covers: LLC formation (~$210–260) and PRO registration (~$50) — this is stated transparently to every pledger
- Once threshold is met, artist is eligible for the event
- Winning the showcase triggers the $1 pledge converting to a $1 investment at the moment the gong rings

**Transparency principle:**

Every Believer knows exactly what their $1 is for before they pledge it. The platform states plainly:

> *"Your $1 pledge covers the cost of forming the legal entity that will hold your stake. The moment this artist is signed — when the gong rings — your pledge converts automatically to a $1 investment in their release LLC. You will be a Founding SteakHolder."*

No ambiguity. No fine print. The pledge is not a donation, not a pre-order, not a goodwill gesture. It is a transparent cost contribution that converts to a real ownership stake at a defined, public moment. The fan knows what they are paying for and what they get in return before they commit a dollar.

### Stage 2 — Investment Raise
*Opens once the artist is signed. Funds actual production.*

- Proper securities offering with full disclosure and investor protections
- Funds: recording, mixing, mastering, music video, photos, branding, merch first run
- Investors receive a **stake in a specific product** (single, album, etc.)
- Multiple investment tiers possible
- The showcase event is the launch moment — invest button appears in real time

---

### The Three Tiers — Believer, Founding SteakHolder, SteakHolder

There are three distinct states a fan or investor passes through. The language should reflect each state precisely — legally, experientially, and in the platform's UI.

| Tier | When | What they are | Legal status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Believer** | During the pledge campaign, before the event | A fan proving demand with $1 | Intent only — not a security, not an investment |
| **Founding SteakHolder** | The moment the gong rings | A pledge that auto-converts to a real stake | Securities holder in the release LLC |
| **SteakHolder** | Anyone who invests after the raise opens | An investor in the release LLC | Securities holder in the release LLC |

**Why the distinction matters:**

Calling a pledge an "investment" before the LLC exists and the raise is open creates securities compliance problems. A pledge is intent — legally, it is not a security. The platform must be precise about this in all pre-event language. The word "Believer" captures the spirit without crossing the regulatory line.

The moment the gong rings, three things happen simultaneously: the contract is awarded, the LLC is confirmed, and the raise goes live. At that moment — and not before — all outstanding pledge credits auto-convert to Founding SteakHolder stakes. The fan's $1 becomes a documented $1 investment in a real legal entity. Their status on the platform updates in real time.

**The conversion is exact and transparent:**

- $1 pledged = $1 invested. No adjustment, no fee, no reduction.
- The LLC formation cost is covered by the aggregate pledge pool — not deducted from individual stakes.
- Every Believer was told this before they pledged. The conversion is not a surprise.
- The platform states clearly at the pledge stage: *"Your $1 will become a $1 stake in this artist's release LLC the moment they are signed."*

**The Founding SteakHolder as a permanent identity:**

Founding SteakHolder status cannot be purchased after the gong rings. It is not available in the open raise. The only way to hold it is to have pledged before the event. This scarcity is structural and permanent — not manufactured.

Every Founding SteakHolder receives:
- A numbered badge (Founding SteakHolder #23) permanently displayed on their profile
- The artist's name, song title, pledge date, and gong date attached to the badge
- Their earnings from that stake tracked and visible alongside the badge
- A documented record of being before the press, before the playlist, before everyone else

**The badge stacks over time:**

A fan who pledges on multiple artists across multiple cycles builds a profile that tells a story. A stack of Founding SteakHolder badges — each with a different artist, a different number, a different date — is a documented record of taste and early belief that no follower count or streaming history can replicate. The platform surfaces this as a **discovery record**: how many artists the fan backed early, and how those artists performed.

*"This SteakHolder has been a Founding SteakHolder on 6 artists. 4 exceeded 60k streams within 6 months of release."*

That record is a credential. It is built entirely by showing up early, consistently. It cannot be faked, bought retroactively, or transferred.

**The event moment:**

When the gong rings, the screen behind the stage updates in real time: the raise is live, and it already shows 300 investors and $300 in it. The open raise does not start at zero. It starts with momentum already built in — every person in the room sees a raise that has believers before it has investors. The psychological effect of that is significant and entirely intentional.

## Investment Structure

### Founding Equity (Pledge Round)
- Small stake in the **artist as an entity** (their LLC)
- Represents belief in the artist's entire future output
- High risk, highest long-term upside
- $1 entry point, fixed small percentage per pledger
- Pledgers receive founding equity — not a donation, not a reward, a stake

### Product Investment (Investment Round)
- Stake in a **specific release** (single, album, tour)
- Cleaner to value, more predictable returns
- Tied to defined revenue streams from that product
- Investors can hold stakes across multiple artist products
- Structured as an LLC per product — each release gets its own entity (precedented via JKBX, SongVest)

### Artist Ownership
- Artist chooses their ownership percentage at onset
- Platform claims a percentage to fund operations, maintenance, and growth
- Remaining percentage is available to investors across both rounds
- Band member splits are documented and locked in the platform before any raise opens

## The Cohort Model — One Year, One Single, One Stage

Every signed artist belongs to an annual cohort. The journey begins at one showcase and concludes at the next. The single they record during that year is released live on stage at the following year's event — in front of the investors who funded it, the fans who followed it, and the new artists who are just beginning their own journey.

### The Heartbeat of the Platform
The annual showcase serves two purposes simultaneously on the same night:
- **Graduation** — last year's cohort performs their finished singles. Investors see their investment culminate. Fans hear the song they waited a year for.
- **Signing** — this year's winners receive their contracts. New pledge raises go live in the room. New investors back the next class.

Every year the event gets richer. Year two has one graduating cohort and one new signing. Year ten has a stage full of legacy artists, a room full of history, and a new class beginning a journey in front of people who have watched it work.

### The Year Mapped

| Month | Stage |
|---|---|
| **Month 1 — Showcase night** | Signed. Pledge raise goes live. Founding equity established. |
| **Month 1–2** | Onboarding complete. LLC formed. All registrations done. Investment raise opens. |
| **Month 2–3** | Production funded. Recording begins. |
| **Month 3–4** | Recording complete. Mixing and mastering. |
| **Month 4–5** | Music video production. |
| **Month 5–6** | Branding, press kit, photos complete. |
| **Month 6–9** | Marketing build. Audience growth. Platform automation running. Social presence growing. |
| **Month 9–10** | Pre-release campaign. Pre-save links live. Press push begins. |
| **Month 10–11** | Single released to all platforms. Earnings begin flowing. |
| **Month 11–12** | Momentum building. Investors watching their stream. |
| **Month 12 — Next Showcase** | Artist performs their single live. Cohort graduates. New cohort signs. |

### What the Cohort Model Does for Each Audience

**Artists** — they are not alone. They move through the year alongside other signed artists at the same stage. Sharing the experience, potentially collaborating, growing audiences together. The cohort becomes a community with a shared identity — a graduating class.

**Investors** — a defined timeline with a known endpoint. They know exactly when the payoff moment arrives — next year's showcase. Not "whenever the artist gets around to it." A specific date on a specific stage. A compelling and clear investment horizon.

**Fans** — a year-long story to follow. They backed an artist at the showcase, they watch the journey unfold over twelve months through platform updates and automated social content, and they are in the room when the single drops at the next event. That emotional investment over a year is deeper than any streaming relationship.

**The platform** — a completely predictable production rhythm. The same structure every year. The team knows exactly what is coming and when. Sponsors know what they are buying. Press knows when to show up.

### Cohorts as a Platform Identity
Each cohort is named and numbered — Class of 2027, Class of 2028. They share an identity on the platform. Investors who backed multiple artists in the same cohort have a portfolio view of that class. Artists in the same cohort can see each other, collaborate, and support each other's raises.

The cohort archive becomes the platform's living history — every class documented, every single released, every journey tracked from showcase night to release night. That archive is the proof of concept compounding year over year. By year five it is the most compelling pitch imaginable to a new investor or a new artist considering applying.

## The Milestone Framework

### The Core Insight
Milestones are universal. The path between them is not. Every artist needs to release music, build an audience, earn money, and grow. The sequence and format of those things varies enormously by artist type. The framework defines milestone categories — the platform assembles the right sequence and format for each artist based on who they are.

### Milestone Categories & Format Options

| Category | Band / Solo | DJ / Electronic | Composer / Producer |
|---|---|---|---|
| **First release** | Single | Track / EP | Composition / recording |
| **Live** | Bar and club shows | Club nights / festival sets | Recital / live session |
| **Physical product** | Band tees, vinyl | Limited vinyl, USB drives, merch | Sheet music, limited pressings |
| **Visual** | Music video | Visualizer / live set film | Performance film |
| **Full length** | Album | Album / mix / compilation | Full composition collection |
| **Major live** | Headlining tour | Festival headliner | Major venue performance |

Same milestones. Different execution. The platform adapts — not the artist.

### The Single Contract — The Universal Recording Agreement
Every recording contract on SoundSteak is a single contract. One song. One investment raise. One defined asset. One LLC. That is the only contract type the platform offers.

**Why this is the right answer:**
- Artists never sign away their future. They commit one song at a time. If the relationship with the platform or investors sours, they are free after that single. No traditional label offers this.
- Investors own a clean, bounded, clearly valued asset. No ambiguity about what the contract covers. They know exactly what they own.
- The legal structure is templated and repeatable. One LLC per single. Each goes through the same Reg A+ process. Scale by repetition, not by complexity.
- The single is the atom. Everything else is built from it.

**The journey is a series of single contracts** building toward something larger. An album is ten singles with a coherent thread. A tour is the live expression of those singles. The artist's catalog on the platform is a portfolio of single contracts — each still active, each still earning, each with its own investor base.

**The early investor story:** An artist's earliest investors backed single one. If that artist becomes enormous, that first single appreciates and those early believers are rewarded for their faith at the ground floor. That is the most compelling investor narrative on the platform — and it is only possible because the single is the universal contract type.

**The destination of every artist journey is a hit single** — not a record deal, not an album advance, not a contract term. A song that earns, that finds its audience, that pays its investors. Everything the platform does is aimed at that outcome for every artist on it.

## The Selection Process & The Event

### The Correct Framing
The event is an **IPO night** — not a competition. By the time artists take the stage they are already signed. The contract is already awarded. The event is the public debut of their offering. The pledge raise going live is the bell ringing. Everything on that stage is a celebration of something already decided.

All selection, voting, and competition happens **online before the event**. No artist performs at a live event only to be cut. The stage is for signed artists only — people who have already earned it through a fully online pre-event process.

### Stage 1 — Application Window
Opens months before the event. Entirely online. Artists submit:
- A video performance
- Connected platform data — streaming numbers, audience size, engagement rate
- Their story and where they are in their journey

A selection panel reviews all submissions and selects a shortlist of 10–15 acts. Shortlisted artists receive a verified badge on their platform profile — recognition that has real value regardless of what comes next.

### Stage 2 — Public Vote
Shortlisted acts are posted on the platform for a two to three week community vote. Fans join the platform to vote for their artists. Artists campaign online. The flywheel is already spinning weeks before the event. The **top five earn a signed contract** and a place on the stage.

The final five are announced publicly on the platform. Their communities are already mobilized. Their pledge raises are already anticipated. By the time the event arrives the room already knows who is performing and why they chose them.

### The Event — Five IPOs on One Night
Five signed artists. Each performs a full set of three to four songs as a **signed act**, not a contestant. During or immediately after each set, their pledge raise goes live. Investors in the room back them on the spot. The emotional pipeline from watching a performance to owning a stake happens in real time.

This is not a talent show. It is five simultaneous public offerings on one stage in one night.

### The Gong
The moment all five signed artists have performed, they gather on stage together. One guest — chosen collectively by all five bands — steps forward and rings a single gong. Every pledge raise opens simultaneously. Every bar starts filling at once.

One strike. Five IPOs. The room goes from silence to chaos.

**Why one ring and not five:**
It unifies the cohort. These artists aren't competing anymore — they're a class. They chose someone together, they stand together, and they launch together. That collective identity is the cohort model made physical in a single moment. One image. One sound. Impossible to forget.

**Who that person is matters enormously.**
The bands chose them collectively — which means all five agreed on one name across different genres, different backgrounds, different audiences. That conversation is already a story worth telling. The announcement of who was chosen and why becomes part of the event itself.

It could be a musician they all grew up listening to. A producer who shaped all of their sounds in different ways. A local legend who represents what the music community means to all of them. Someone with no industry profile — a music teacher, a venue owner who gave them all their first show. The right answer will reveal itself when real artists are in the room making that decision. That's as it should be.

**This is the defining image of the platform** — the entire signing class on stage together, the top five performing, one guest, one gong, one swing. That photograph gets shared everywhere the next morning. It becomes what SoundSteak looks like before anyone can describe what SoundSteak is.

### What Signing Unlocks
- Artist is formally signed to the label
- Pledge raise activates publicly on event night
- Platform begins onboarding infrastructure
- Cohort year begins — the journey toward a finished single at next year's event
- **AI licensing terms are set** — the artist defines their position on AI use at signing, documented in the contract and enforced by the platform (see Artist Rights in the AI Era)

### Live Shows, Revenue, and the 360 Deal Line

SoundSteak does not take a cut of independent live show revenue. The LLC covers one single — the recording, its streaming income, sync placements, merch tied to the release, and AI licensing. The artist's live career is theirs. What they do on their own tour, in their own markets, with their own booking agent is outside the contract's scope entirely.

This is a deliberate structural choice, not an oversight. Taking a percentage of touring income is the defining feature of a 360 deal — the thing the music industry has done to artists for two decades and that artists most resent. SoundSteak's entire positioning is built against that model. The moment the platform reaches into live income, it becomes a variation of the thing it replaced.

**The platform's live revenue comes from its own events.** Event One and Event Two are SoundSteak productions — ticketed, sponsored, and documented. The platform earns from those events as a producer, not as a rights holder in the music. That separation is clean and defensible.

**Live performance obligation is fulfilled through platform events, not enforced externally.** Rather than requiring a minimum number of independent shows — which creates an enforcement problem the platform cannot solve — the contract requires participation in SoundSteak's own events. Every signed artist appears at Event One and Event Two in some capacity. This is already part of the structure. The obligation enforces itself.

**What "appearing" means for non-performing artists:** At Event One, all signed artists are present when the class is announced and the gong rings — their story is being told, their profile is live, their raise is open. At Event Two, the entire graduating cohort is represented — not just the artists who perform, but every release from that cycle. Press covers the event, not just the headliner. Fans discover artists they didn't come for. An artist doesn't need to be on the stage to be part of the night.

This also solves the multi-song problem entirely. A live show is the artist's full catalog — not just the single the LLC owns. Asking for a cut of that would require attribution across every song performed, which is administratively impossible and philosophically wrong. SoundSteak's events are built around the single. That's the right context for the platform's live presence.

### Artists at the Event Who Don't Perform
All artists being signed in a given cycle are invited to Event One. Only the top five — ranked by pledge support — perform. If one of the top five cannot perform, the next artist in line takes the slot. The stage is never empty. This is not a consolation structure — every signed artist stands in the room when the class is announced, when the contracts are awarded, and when the gong rings. They are part of the moment. They simply don't have a set.

### Artists Who Didn't Get Signed
They were not invited to the event — they competed entirely online. They receive a shortlist badge if they made it that far. They have a platform profile, a growing audience from the campaign, and a stronger application for next year. They were not promised a stage and did not lose one — they simply continue building toward next year.

## Showcase Cadence & Growth Pacing

### The Model — Annual, By Design

**Once per year. That's the answer.**

Scarcity creates desire. A quarterly showcase is a local gig. An annual showcase is a cultural event — something the industry marks on the calendar, something artists prepare for months in advance, something fans and investors anticipate the way they anticipate SXSW or the Grammys.

The reference point is the **NPR Tiny Desk Concert series** — culturally significant precisely because not everyone gets one. Artists bring their absolute best because the opportunity is rare and the stakes are real.

**What annual exclusivity does for each audience:**
- **Artists** — a full year to prepare, build their following, and make their submission the best possible version of themselves. Being selected means something.
- **Fans** — appointment viewing. A specific date. A cultural moment people talk about and count down to.
- **Investors** — deal flow is concentrated and curated. Once a year, the best emerging artists present to their community. Exclusivity makes the investment feel considered and prestigious.
- **Operations** — one extraordinary event per year is achievable at the highest level of quality. Twelve months to properly support each signed class through their milestone progression before the next class arrives.

**The annual showcase needs its own name** — something that stands alone as a cultural brand the way Tiny Desk or SXSW does. Open creative question to be developed.

### Regional Expansion Path
Once the annual model is proven, scale by adding regional qualifying events that feed the national showcase — not replacing it. Regional showcases become the farm system. The national showcase remains the Super Bowl. Regional winners earn a spot at the national event.

### Community-Driven Local Events — Future Possibility

Not a planned feature. A flagged possibility for when the platform has reached sufficient scale in a given market.

The fan intelligence layer already knows where SteakHolder density is building. If a city like Chicago accumulates hundreds of verified investors backing multiple artists from the same cohort, the platform has everything it needs to recognize that a local event would perform — without anyone having to manufacture that demand. The Cue system surfaces it: enough SteakHolders in the same market expressing interest in a live moment, and the platform can ask if there's appetite to organize one.

These wouldn't be SoundSteak events imposed from the top down. They'd emerge from the data and be organized through the platform's existing infrastructure — attribution tracking, verified profiles, Cue-based RSVPs. A room full of investors in the same city who already have documented relationships with the same artists is a natural gathering. The platform just recognizes it and gives it a shape.

**When this makes sense:** After the annual model is proven, after multiple cohort years have built SteakHolder communities in multiple cities, and after the platform has the operational capacity to support events it doesn't physically produce. Not before.

### The Second Door — Application Track
The showcase is not the only path onto the platform forever. Once the model is established, artists with a verified existing following above a defined threshold can apply directly — skip the showcase, go straight to a pledge raise. This prevents the platform's growth from being bottlenecked by one annual event.

**Showcase** = discovery and launch for emerging, unknown artists
**Application track** = fast lane for artists who already have an audience

Both paths lead to the same onboarding process.

### Event Night Structure

The event runs 2–3 hours. Every element is deliberate and tight.

**Run of show (rough order):**
1. The gong rings — once, to open the show. This is the moment all platform sales switch on simultaneously: investment raises, merch pre-orders, everything. The host or the chosen guest rings it. The room knows what it means.
2. Host intro — sets the stage, frames the night, explains briefly what the audience just witnessed with the gong.
3. Each performing artist — one speech, one song, ~10 minutes per slot. The speech comes first: why this song, what this moment means, where they came from. Then one song, chosen deliberately. Not a set — a statement. The constraint is the point.
4. Awards — host brings recipients on stage. Awards recognize the people behind the artists: a teacher, a venue owner, an early fan, a community figure. Not industry trophies. Human moments.
5. Closing remarks — the host closes. Simple. Don't overstay.

**The one-song rule:** Each performing artist plays exactly one song. The constraint is intentional — it forces the artist to choose which song defines this moment, and makes every performance feel like a considered statement rather than a casual set. One perfect song lands harder than three.

Ideally, the song performed is the single they are about to record — the thing the room is about to invest in. It doesn't have to be finished. It may not even be written yet. That's allowed. The performance is a declaration of direction, not a finished product. If the song exists, it's a preview. If it doesn't, the performance is proof of what's coming.

**The live data screen:** The platform's real-time data is visible in the room throughout the event — SteakHolder counts climbing, raise percentages filling, pre-order numbers moving. The audience can watch the market responding to each performance in real time. Every artist who plays knows their set is moving those numbers while they're on stage.

### Ticket Structure

**SteakHolders** — presale access and presale pricing. The earliest and most loyal supporters see the tickets before anyone else and pay less for them. This is a tangible, recurring benefit of being a SteakHolder beyond financial returns.

**Award recipients** — invited as guests at no charge. If you're being honored, there is no ticket price. They are guests of the platform.

**General public and new investors** — everyone is welcome. The event is not closed or exclusive to existing SteakHolders. New attendees are potential future investors, and every event is a customer acquisition moment. Open doors, general admission pricing.

The event is intentionally open. The benefit for SteakHolders is priority and price — not exclusivity.

## Artist Eligibility & Verification

### The Real Criteria
"Performing artists only" was the original framing — intended to exclude AI-generated acts. But as written it accidentally excludes a large and legitimate category of musician. A techno DJ, a beatmaker, an electronic producer, a composer — all real humans making real music with real audiences. The format of performance is not the point.

The actual gatekeeping criteria:
1. **A verifiable human identity** behind the music
2. **A real audience** — provable through connected platform data
3. **Original music** — not covers-only, not AI-generated
4. **Active creative output** — not a dormant catalog being passively monetized

Whether that human performs on a stage with a guitar or behind a booth with a laptop is irrelevant to eligibility.

### Who Is Eligible
- Bands and solo artists in any genre
- DJs and electronic artists — a DJ set is a live performance; festival and club touring is as legitimate as any band on a stage
- Beatmakers and producers releasing music under their own name — Flying Lotus, J Dilla, Four Tet
- Electronic, techno, and ambient producers — including those who perform live sets behind a laptop and controller
- Composers releasing original work under their own creative identity
- Artists who primarily release recorded music without heavy touring — audience size and engagement are the proof, not tour schedules

### The AI Red Line — Where the Platform Draws It
The exclusion of AI music is not about the tools an artist uses. It is about whether a human creative identity is the verifiable source of the work. This distinction matters legally, ethically, and for investor protection.

**The three problems with fully AI-generated music:**
1. **Ownership is legally unresolved.** The US Copyright Office has repeatedly ruled that AI-generated works without sufficient human creative input cannot be copyrighted. If the music cannot be copyrighted it cannot be owned, and if it cannot be owned investors cannot hold a stake in it. Uncopyrightable work is uninvestable.
2. **Authenticity is the platform's foundation.** The entire trust model — for artists, investors, and the industry — rests on real human creativity. AI-generated music undermines the story for every other artist on the platform.
3. **Valuation is impossible.** AI music is infinitely replicable with no inherent scarcity. A human producer's beat has value partly because of who made it and the creative decisions behind it. That value does not exist in AI-generated work.

**The practical test:**
Is there a human musician whose creative identity is the verifiable source of the music? Can that human be credited, verified, and held accountable for the work? Is the underlying music copyrightable under current law? If yes to all three — eligible. If the music is AI-generated with no sufficient human authorship — excluded.

**AI-assisted is not the same as AI-generated.** A producer who uses AI tools as part of their creative process — the way a photographer uses Photoshop or a musician uses a DAW — but who makes the substantive creative decisions is a human creator. The line is: does a human musician make the creative choices, or does the AI?

### The Vocalist / Performer Scenario
A performer who is not a musician but sings or performs over produced music is a legitimate artist and eligible — with conditions.

**Over human-made purchased beats:** Fully eligible. Hip-hop and R&B have operated this way for decades. The beat is licensed IP. The vocal performance is original. The finished recording is a real creative work. The beat producer either needs to be in the platform with their stake documented, or the beat must be purchased outright with no ongoing revenue obligation to an outside party.

**Over human-made beats with outside revenue shares:** Eligible only if the deal closes inside the platform. If a beat producer holds a revenue share claim that lives outside the system that is a leak investors cannot see or account for. Solution: an internal beat marketplace where producers list beats for sale or license, transactions happen inside the system, and any revenue share is automatically routed.

**Over AI-assisted human production:** Eligible. Human creative identity is the verifiable source.

**Over fully AI-generated music:** Not eligible. Uncopyrightable, unownable, not investable. This is the red line.

**The live performer with no recordings:** Conditionally eligible for the showcase — but must produce original recordings with human-made music to progress beyond it.

### Eligibility Matrix

| Artist Situation | Eligible | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Band or solo artist, original music | Yes | Standard path |
| DJ performing live sets | Yes | Live performance is valid |
| Electronic / techno producer | Yes | Human authorship required |
| Vocalist over purchased human-made beats | Yes | Beat deal must be in system |
| Vocalist over licensed human beats with revenue share | Yes | Revenue share must be in system |
| Vocalist over AI-assisted human production | Yes | Human creative identity is source |
| Vocalist over fully AI-generated music | No | Uncopyrightable, uninvestable |
| Ghost producer under fake persona | No | No verifiable human identity |
| AI-generated act | No | No human creative identity |
| Major label artist without master ownership | Conditional | Legal clarity required first |

### Who Is Excluded
- AI-generated acts with no verifiable human creative identity
- Purely algorithmic music with no human authorship
- Ghost producers operating under a fake persona with no real audience
- Content farms generating music for streaming manipulation
- Artists whose masters are owned by a major label in a way that prevents them from legally offering investor stakes — needs legal clarity before those artists can participate

### How Verification Works
The auth connection requirement is the primary verification mechanism. A real artist has:
- A legitimate Spotify for Artists account with genuine streaming history that predates their application
- Real social accounts with real engagement over time
- A provable audience that cannot be manufactured overnight

These are difficult to fake at scale. The live showcase adds a final layer for the annual event — but connected platform data is the foundation of verification for all artists on all paths.

### Showcase Format for Non-Traditional Artists
The annual showcase must accommodate the full range of eligible artists:
- A DJ set in a venue is as valid as a band on a stage
- A produced video submission is valid for electronic artists who don't perform in traditional formats
- A live-recorded studio session is valid for composers and producers
- The submission format is flexible — the authenticity of the human and audience behind it is not

## Release Formats & The Compilation Path

SoundSteak is single-first by design — one LLC, one raise, one release. That is the atomic unit. But the platform is not the album killer. It is the system that makes every format possible on the artist's terms, funded through the same infrastructure, executed by the same professional network, and tracked through the same revenue pipe.

### The Compilation / EP / Album Path

An artist who has released multiple singles through SoundSteak accumulates a catalog of documented, legally clean, SteakHolder-backed recordings. At any point, they can declare a compilation — a formal bundling of existing singles into an EP, album, or collection — as its own release event.

**How it works mechanically:**

The compilation is a new release entity (its own LLC or a licensing arrangement between existing LLCs — legal framework TBD). It draws from the catalog of individually released tracks. Existing SteakHolders in each contributing single retain their stakes in those individual LLCs and gain proportional recognition in the compilation's documentation. New investment may be raised specifically for the compilation's production, packaging, and marketing campaign — with its own founding cohort if the platform determines a raise is warranted.

The compilation has its own milestones, its own release cycle, its own Cue sequences for governance and mediation. It is a first-class release, not a side product.

**What triggers a compilation:**

- Artist discretion — they decide when their catalog is ready to be presented as a body of work
- A defined number of released singles (e.g., 3–5) that form a coherent arc
- SteakHolder governance vote — existing investors may propose a compilation as a milestone
- A sync or licensing opportunity that requires a full-length submission

**The EP as a distinct format:**

An EP (typically 3–6 tracks) sits between a single and an album. It may include previously released singles plus one or two unreleased tracks recorded in the same cycle. The platform supports this naturally: existing track LLCs contribute their recordings, new tracks can be raised and recorded under a companion raise, and the EP LLC packages everything together.

### Merch Cadence — One Drop at a Time

SoundSteak merch is not a store. It is a rhythm. Every artist on the platform ships on the same cadence — one product released as a pre-sale every two to three months, for the duration of their time on the platform. Each drop is its own event: a Cue sequence, a SteakHolder notification, a new reason to share the artist.

The goal is not to maximize inventory. It is to maximize moments. Four drops per year means four separate windows where the platform has something to push, SteakHolders have something to share, and the release LLC has a new revenue line opening. That compounds across a growing roster.

**Year 1 cadence — every artist, at signing:**

Every artist begins with the same three founding products, released in sequence across the year. A fourth drop closes the year with event ticket pre-sales.

| Drop | Timing | Product | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | Month 1–2 after signing | Digital bundle | Lossless audio, lyrics, liner notes PDF, production notes. Zero production cost. Immediate earn. |
| **2** | Month 3–4 | Zine pre-sale | 30-day pre-order window. Print-to-order through platform-contracted printer. Numbered run. |
| **3** | Month 5–6 | Shirt pre-sale | One design, one colorway. 30-day window. Print-on-demand — no inventory risk, no minimum. |
| **4** | Month 10–11 | Event ticket pre-sale | SteakHolders get first-window access before public. The year ends with the room filling up. |

The digital bundle drops first because it costs nothing to produce and earns immediately. The zine and shirt follow at pace. Tickets close the year — every artist's last drop is the platform's biggest event.

**The merch catalog grows with the catalog:**

Each year an artist stays on the platform, new products unlock — not on a fixed schedule, but as the catalog, the audience, and the streaming numbers warrant. The platform proposes. SteakHolders vote. The professional network executes.

| Songs on Platform | Merch Unlocked |
|---|---|
| **1 song (Year 1)** | Digital bundle, zine, shirt, event tickets |
| **2 songs (Year 2)** | 45rpm vinyl — one song per side. First physical artifact. The format was built for exactly this. |
| **3 songs (Year 3)** | Cassette or 10" EP, 3-track digital bundle upgrade, possible collab merch |
| **4 songs (Year 4)** | Audience large enough for brand partnership conversations. Limited collab drops. |
| **5 songs (Year 5)** | Compilation LP viable — vinyl, zine companion, box set if numbers support it |

Every new format is a governance moment — SteakHolders see the streaming data, vote on whether to greenlight the run, and become the first pre-order window when it opens.

**The 45rpm as a milestone:**

Two songs on the platform means a 45rpm single is viable — one track per side, the format that defined the single for fifty years. It is not nostalgia. It is the right format at the right moment: small run, affordable to press, collector-grade artifact, and a meaningful signal that the artist has a body of work worth holding in your hands. The 45 is the first vinyl product most artists on the platform will release. It should feel like an event.

**Every drop is a platform event:**

The cadence is not artist-by-artist. It is platform-wide. When multiple artists are shipping on the same two-to-three month rhythm, the platform always has something happening. A new digital bundle, a new zine window, a new shirt drop, a vinyl governance vote in progress. SoundSteak is never quiet. Every SteakHolder on the platform, regardless of which artists they've backed, is always inside an active release window for someone.

**The album timeline:**

A standard album is 10–12 tracks. At one single per year, that is a decade-long catalog built on the platform — every track documented, funded, and earning before the album exists. The album on SoundSteak is not a gamble. It is a victory lap on a proven body of work.

### Artist Rights in the AI Era

### The Three-Layer Context Engine

**Layer 1 — Profile Tasks with Compensation**

Contextual data requests surface on the artist's dashboard as completable tasks — exactly like milestones. Each task has a label, a plain-language prompt, a response format (text, voice note, or AI interview), and a payout drawn from the context fund.

The artist sees tasks like:

> **"Describe your drum arrangement"** — Walk us through the percussion on this track. What is the kick pattern doing? What did you leave out on purpose? → *$12.00 from the context fund*

> **"Annotate your bridge"** — What is the bridge doing emotionally that the verse and chorus don't? Why did this section need to exist? → *$8.00*

> **"Your primary influence on this song"** — Name one artist whose work directly shaped how you approached this single. Describe specifically what you borrowed from them. → *$6.00*

> **"Where were you when you wrote this?"** — Not just geography. What was the environment, the time of day, the emotional state. → *$5.00*

> **"Production reference"** — What song did you tell your producer to make this feel like, and what specifically were you pointing at — the tonal quality, the arrangement, the vocal treatment, the space? → *$7.00*

> **"Lyric intent"** — Pick one line from the chorus and explain what it means to you. Is it autobiographical, narrative, or metaphorical? → *$5.00*

The **context fund** is a ring-fenced portion of developer console revenue — the commercial users of the data fund the collection of it. The more the console earns, the more tasks get funded. Artists who complete more tasks have richer profiles that command higher per-use rates in the console. SteakHolders have a direct financial interest in their artist completing every task — richer context means higher licensing revenue flowing through the LLC. The incentive is completely self-reinforcing.

Uncompleted tasks stay on the dashboard indefinitely. They do not expire. An artist who completes a task two years after the release still enriches the catalog and still gets paid for it. The profile is never finished. It just keeps getting richer.

**Layer 2 — The AI Interview**

Not every artist thinks in structured fields. A prompt like "describe your chord progression" produces a blank stare from half the musicians alive. The AI interview solves this.

Instead of forms, the platform hands the artist to a conversational AI — potentially the same AI partner whose data needs shaped the questions being asked — that conducts the interview the way a skilled music journalist would. The artist talks. The AI listens, asks follow-ups, and extracts structured data from natural conversation.

> *"Tell me about the chord progression in the chorus — I'm hearing something unusual in the second chord. What were you going for there?"*

> *"You mentioned the song is about your father. Is that explicit in the lyrics or more of a feeling underneath them?"*

> *"You said the production reference was Phoebe Bridgers. Was it the tonal quality, the sparse arrangement, the vocal treatment, or something else?"*

> *"What did you leave out of this song that you almost kept?"*

The output is not a transcript. It is structured, tagged, queryable data — genre markers, emotional descriptors, production notes, lyrical annotations, influence relationships — extracted from natural conversation and mapped to profile fields automatically. The artist never sees a spreadsheet. They just had a conversation about something they care about deeply.

This is a natural extension of the Voice Layer already at the core of the platform. The platform's conversational intelligence already knows who every artist is, where they are in their journey, what their release looks like, and what their SteakHolders need to know. Turning that same interface toward contextual data collection is a mode, not a new system.

**If an AI company is the engine running these interviews** — optimized to extract exactly the data fields their training pipeline needs, structured in the format their models consume — the partnership stops being a licensing deal and becomes a product collaboration. The AI partner shapes the questions. The platform delivers the answers. The catalog that results is the most AI-native music dataset in existence, built from the ground up in collaboration with the company that will use it.

### Services-for-Equity Arrangements

Some professionals — recording studios, mastering engineers, mixing engineers, videographers, photographers — may be willing to take a percentage of the release LLC instead of an upfront cash payment. This is a standard arrangement in early-stage industries (common in tech, film, and independent production) and fits cleanly into the SoundSteak structure. The LLC already exists to receive equity investment. The professional's contribution is just a different form of consideration: labor and expertise instead of cash.

**Why this matters to both sides:**

For artists with limited cash: top-quality collaborators become accessible without requiring the raise to fully fund every service upfront. The recording doesn't have to wait until every dollar is raised.

For professionals: they gain a stake in the music's long-term success — streams, sync, live, AI licensing, all of it. A mastering engineer who took 1.5% of a single that eventually places in a major sync is earning from that decision for years. Their work and their upside are permanently linked.

**How it works mechanically:**

1. The professional lists their services-for-equity availability on their profile — an opt-in signal that they're open to the arrangement.
2. The artist browses professionals who have opted in and initiates a negotiation Cue — the platform sends the professional an offer specifying the percentage proposed in exchange for their service.
3. The professional accepts, declines, or counter-proposes. If they counter, the Cue loop continues. If agreement isn't reached, the platform surfaces a mediation workflow — the same kind used for band split disputes.
4. Once both parties agree, each signs through their profiles via the platform's Cue system. No separate email chain. No external contract wrangling.
5. The LLC issues the professional their equity stake. It appears on their profile the same way cash-backed stakes appear for SteakHolders.

**What their profile shows:**

Their SoundSteak profile is the only place in the industry where a collaborator's credit and their investment stake appear in the same verified record. "I mixed this record" and "I own 1.5% of what it earns" are the same entry. The platform documents both at the same moment because they're the same transaction.

**Profile dual-role:**

These professionals are simultaneously a credited collaborator and a SteakHolder. Their role tags reflect both capacities. They appear in searches for mixing engineers AND as an investor in that artist's release. Their earnings dashboard shows service income from other projects alongside equity income from this one.

**The alignment effect:**

A videographer who owns 2% of the release LLC has a different relationship to the music video they're about to shoot than one who gets paid flat and moves on. Professionals with a stake in the outcome work differently. SoundSteak makes that alignment contractual and automatic — not dependent on personal goodwill or side agreements that don't survive a business relationship changing.

**Legal note — requires review:**

Services-for-equity is not a cash investment and likely falls outside Reg CF. The mechanism for issuing equity to a professional in exchange for services (valuation of services, LLC unit issuance, tax treatment for both parties) needs legal guidance. The platform should have a standard template for this arrangement — similar to the template operating agreement — reviewed by a music and startup attorney before offering it. Key questions: how are the services valued? Is the equity a separate class from cash investor equity, or the same class? What are the 1099 or W-2 implications for the professional? These are solvable but must be defined before the arrangement goes live.

## Governance & Shareholder Voting

Governance operates on two distinct tiers — artist-level and platform-level. Both are public, both are binding within their defined scope, and both treat stakeholders as genuine owners rather than passive participants. This is one of the platform's most differentiated features and one of its most powerful trust builders.

### Two Tiers of Governance

**Tier 1 — Artist-Level Votes**
Shareholders in a specific artist vote on decisions that affect that artist. Scoped to people with skin in that particular game.

**Tier 2 — Platform-Level Votes**
The broader stakeholder community votes on how SoundSteak itself evolves. Regular, structured asks for participation in shaping the platform. Investors are not just capital — they are co-owners of the system their money runs through, and their input is actively solicited.

| Decision Type | Who Votes | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Artist creative direction | Artist investors | Collab choices, merch designs, tour routing, release timing |
| Artist business decisions | Artist investors | Next single theme options, video concept options |
| Product roadmap | All active investors | Which features get built next, what gets prioritized |
| Policy changes | All members | Eligibility criteria, AI redline policies |
| Platform economics | All investors | Fee structure adjustments, new revenue sharing models |
| Showcase format | All members | How the annual event evolves, new categories |
| New markets | All investors | Geographic expansion, new artist types |
| Community standards | All members | Code of conduct, moderation, dispute resolution |

### The Cadence of Platform Governance
Not every decision goes to a vote — that creates paralysis. The platform team operates day to day. But on defined categories of significant decisions, votes are called on a regular rhythm with clear timelines, clear options, and honest explanation of tradeoffs.

**Quarterly platform governance moments** — scheduled, anticipated, participatory. Investors know when they are coming. They look forward to them. They feel like owners because they are.

### Why Asking Investors to Shape the Platform Is Strategic
- Converts passive capital providers into active platform owners
- Provides the most valuable product research possible — asking people whose money is in the system what they need
- Perfect incentive alignment — a better platform means better artist outcomes means better returns
- Makes investors feel respected, heard, and invested beyond just their financial stake
- Creates a community of engaged stakeholders who advocate for the platform because they helped build it

### Live Votes vs. Cues

**Live votes** — happen during the annual showcase in real time. Audience votes on performances. Fast, emotional, public, immediate. Results are visible as they happen.

**Governance votes** — asynchronous, deliberate, time-bounded. Artists propose options, investors consider and vote over a defined window. Results are documented publicly. These shape decisions that matter over weeks and months, not minutes.

Both are part of the same governance philosophy — that the people with skin in the game deserve a voice in the outcome.

### How Collab Voting Works
Shareholders vote on who the artist collaborates with next — producer, featured artist, songwriter, director, engineer. The mechanics protect the artist while giving investors a genuine voice:

1. Artist nominates a shortlist of collaborators they are actually willing to work with — they are never forced into a collaboration they don't want
2. Shareholders vote from that shortlist
3. Winning collaborator receives a platform invite notification
4. If already in the system — collab is proposed immediately
5. If not in the system — they receive the "your name came up in a shareholder vote" invite, pulling them into the ecosystem

This creates a secondary social layer — shareholders campaigning for their preferred collab, discussing options, sharing the vote. Organic content the platform didn't have to manufacture.

### Voteable Decisions
- Who to collaborate with next — producer, featured artist, songwriter, director
- Next single theme or direction — from artist-defined options
- Merch designs — artist submits options, shareholders choose
- Tour routing — which cities to prioritize
- Release timing — from artist-defined windows
- Music video concept — from artist-defined treatments

### What Cannot Be Voted On
- Creative content of the music itself — the artist retains full creative control
- Band membership — who is in the band is not a shareholder decision
- Personal decisions — voting scope is strictly professional and business-facing

### The AI Redline Governance Model
Rather than a fixed written policy on AI creative content — which would be obsoleted by rapidly evolving technology — the platform uses an AI evaluation layer to assess each submission on a case by case basis.

**How it works:**
- Every submission is analyzed for degree of human creative authorship
- The assessment is a spectrum — not a binary pass/fail
- The AI evaluates melodic origin, arrangement decisions, production choices, and the degree to which human creative intent drove the output
- The system flags its assessment and confidence level
- Clear cases are handled automatically
- Edge cases go to a human review panel that includes working musicians
- Artists can appeal any ruling to the human panel

**Why AI decides the AI question:**
- The technology moves faster than any written policy can keep up with
- The evaluation system can be updated as tools evolve without rewriting platform rules
- Assessments are consistent, transparent, and explainable
- Removes the platform from being a subjective arbiter of a culturally charged debate
- Artists who disagree have something specific to appeal against

**Human oversight is non-negotiable:** The AI recommends. The human panel has final say on all contested cases. Working musicians must be represented on that panel.

### Governance Principles
- Voting power is proportional to ownership stake
- The artist always retains creative veto — governance is advisory on creative matters, binding only on defined business decisions
- Scope of voting rights is legally defined in the band agreement and investor disclosure at the point of investment
- Governance model must not create employer/employee relationships or co-producer liability — legal review required

### The Spirit of the Cue

Governance votes are not a compliance checkbox. They are one of the primary ways SteakHolders experience being part of something. A vote that shows up in someone's inbox should feel worth opening — surprising, meaningful, occasionally fun.

The list of things to vote on will grow as the platform grows. Artist milestones, platform decisions, and community-shaped choices will all generate vote moments. The exact items are not fully predetermined — they emerge from what the platform is actually doing. A few principles for what makes a good Cue:

- It should feel like it matters. The outcome changes something real.
- It should be fast to participate in. No essays required.
- It should occasionally be delightful. Not every vote is about fee structures.
- It should arrive on a cadence that builds habit, not one that burns people out.
- The result should always be published transparently — and the platform should acknowledge what the community decided, not quietly implement it.

Governance is a living system. Add to this list constantly as the platform generates new moments worth putting to a vote.

## Platform Structure & Governance Over the Platform Itself

### Legal Structure — For-Profit by Necessity, Accountable by Design
The platform must be a for-profit entity. This is not a philosophical choice — it is a legal requirement. Regulation A+ securities offerings can only be issued by for-profit companies. A nonprofit cannot offer investment stakes to the public. The legal structure is for-profit.

What the legal structure does not determine is how the platform behaves, what it prioritizes, or how accountable it is to the people who fund it. A for-profit company can be run with the values of a public utility. That is the intent.

The right structural analogy is a **benefit corporation** — a legal for-profit entity that has a fiduciary duty to stakeholders beyond just shareholders. A benefit corporation can explicitly prioritize artist welfare, investor transparency, and community health alongside financial returns. It can be sued for failing to uphold those commitments — not just for failing to maximize profit. This is the structure that matches the platform's values while preserving the legal standing to operate as described.

### Content Ownership — The Legal Foundation of the Investment
For investors to hold a stake in a recording's earnings, a legal entity must own those rights and offer them. This is why each single gets its own LLC. The platform holds a documented stake in each LLC — not full ownership, but a defined percentage that gives it legal standing to structure the Reg A+ offering and act as the administrative entity for the investment.

The artist retains their agreed ownership percentage. Investors purchase their stake from the LLC. The platform's percentage covers its role as label, administrator, and infrastructure provider. Everything is documented, filed, and public. The platform cannot offer what it does not own a stake in — which is precisely why the ownership structure must be airtight at signing before any raise opens.

### Investor Governance Over the Platform — The Most Radical Accountability Mechanism
The same governance architecture that gives SteakHolders a voice in artist decisions extends to the platform itself — including the compensation of the people who run it.

Investors can vote to set compensation limits for platform executives. The people who fund this system have the right to determine how much of its revenue flows to the team at the top versus flowing back to artists, investors, and the ecosystem participants the platform exists to serve.

This is the founding principle applied inward. The system that prevents unfair extraction from artists applies equally to the founders and executives. No one is exempt from the transparency that defines everything else. The music industry's historical model is label executives extracting enormous value while artists struggle. This platform makes that model structurally impossible — not just for artists, but for itself.

**What this signals to every participant:**
- To artists: the people running this platform cannot get rich off your work at your expense
- To investors: your capital cannot be consumed by executive excess — there are limits, and you set them
- To the industry: this is what a label looks like when it is accountable to the people it serves rather than to its own enrichment

### The Compensation Governance Mechanism
- Platform executive compensation is disclosed publicly — total compensation, broken down by role
- SteakHolders vote on compensation caps at defined intervals — annually at minimum
- Caps are binding — not advisory
- Any compensation above the voted cap requires a new vote to authorize
- The platform's operating percentage is itself subject to governance review — investors can vote to reduce it if the platform is profitable enough to operate at a lower take

This does not mean the platform's team goes unpaid or that talent cannot be attracted. It means the community of people whose money and work power the platform have a real, binding voice in how that platform compensates the people they trust to run it. That trust is earned continuously — not assumed at founding and never revisited.

## Platform Revenue Model

### The Non-Negotiable Operating Percentage
Before any distribution happens — before artists receive their cut, before investors see dividends, before fans earn from ad revenue — the platform takes its defined operating percentage off the top. This is not subject to a Cue. It is not adjustable by investor preference. It is the fixed cost of the system existing at all.

This percentage covers:
- Staff compensation — the team required to run, maintain, and grow the platform
- Technical infrastructure — servers, integrations, security, platform development
- Legal and compliance — ongoing securities compliance, contract maintenance, regulatory requirements
- Event production — the annual showcase does not fund itself
- Operating reserves — the minimum runway buffer that keeps the platform solvent through lean periods

**Why this must be non-negotiable:**
A platform that can be voted into underfunding is a platform that can be voted into failure. Investors who vote to reduce the operating percentage below functional levels are voting to destroy the thing their investment depends on. The non-negotiable floor protects them from themselves — and from any coordinated effort to destabilize the platform by starving it of operational resources.

The percentage is set conservatively at founding, disclosed publicly from day one, and reviewed periodically as the platform scales. As revenue grows and fixed costs represent a smaller proportion of total income, the percentage may decrease — but only through a deliberate platform decision, not through governance pressure.

### What Governance Can Influence
Above the non-negotiable operating floor, governance does have a voice:

## Legal & Regulatory

### Two Completely Separate Investment Vehicles

SoundSteak operates two distinct investment structures that must never be conflated — in legal filings, in public communications, or in investor conversations.

**Vehicle 1 — Investment in SoundSteak the company (platform equity)**

This is equity in the platform itself. Investors own a stake in the business that runs everything. This is what the seed round is. Separate legal entity, separate regulatory path.

- **Reg D 506(b)** — current seed round path. Private placement. Accredited investors only (net worth $1M+ or income $200K+), plus up to 35 sophisticated non-accredited investors. No advertising or public promotion. No cap on amount raised. Must file Form D with the SEC within 15 days of first sale. This is what the teaser/seed page feeds into — the form connects to a private conversation, not a public offering.
- **Reg D 506(c)** — allows general solicitation and public advertising, but still accredited investors only with active verification. Relevant if the seed page is shared publicly — worth flagging with a securities attorney before broad promotion.
- **Reg A+ Tier 2** — the path to public company equity. Up to $75M/year, open to any investor. Requires SEC qualification (months of process, $50K–$150K+ in legal and accounting). The "always publicly available" equity model. A Year 3+ conversation — not viable until the platform has traction to show the SEC.

**Vehicle 2 — Investment in individual artist release LLCs (product equity)**

This is equity in a specific song. Investors own a stake in the LLC formed around one release. Completely separate from platform equity. Different entity, different securities offering, different regulatory path.

- **Reg CF** — crowdfunding via FINRA-registered funding portal (Wefunder, Republic, StartEngine). Up to $5M per 12-month period. Open to any investor regardless of accredited status. Annual reporting requirements. The correct vehicle for the fan investment model — low barrier, any investor, platform handles compliance through the portal partner.
- **Reg A+** — alternative for artist LLCs if individual raises exceed $5M (unlikely at early scale, but possible for established artists). Same SEC qualification process as above but per LLC.

**The rule:** Never mix the two in investor communications. A SteakHolder buying into an artist LLC owns nothing in SoundSteak the company. A seed investor in SoundSteak the company owns nothing in any artist LLC. These are separate products, separate risks, separate returns.

### The Precedent — What Existing Platforms Did
This is not uncharted territory. Multiple platforms have navigated this and their SEC filings are public record — a free roadmap.

- **SongVest** — received the first-ever SEC qualification of music earnings interests as securities, using **Regulation A+**. The foundational precedent.
- **JKBX (Jukebox)** — filed extensively under Reg A+, publicly stated that their shares are securities. Form 1-A filings on SEC EDGAR are a detailed structural blueprint.
- **Musicow** — additional Reg A+ filer with public filings on SEC EDGAR.
- **Royalty Flow** — used Reg A+ under Title IV of the JOBS Act.

All public filings searchable at [SEC EDGAR](https://www.sec.gov/cgi-bin/browse-edgar).

### The Path Forward — Regulation A+
Reg A+ (Title IV of the JOBS Act) allows companies to raise up to **$75 million per year** from the general public — not just accredited investors. This is the correct path based on precedent.

**Structure used by existing platforms:**
- Create an LLC that holds the earnings rights to a specific song or product
- Sell membership interests in that LLC to investors
- Investors receive their pro-rata share of earnings that flow into the LLC
- Each product release gets its own LLC — clean, bounded, precedented

**Key advantage:** Reg A+ is available to everyday investors, not just wealthy accredited investors. This fits the fan-investment model perfectly.

### The Path to Avoid
- Registering as an **Investment Adviser** under the Investment Advisers Act — expensive, complex, ongoing compliance burden
- Registering under the **Investment Company Act** — even more burdensome
- **Regulation D** — limits participation to accredited investors only, which kills the fan-investment model

### The Björn Ulvaeus Connection — Credits Due
Björn Ulvaeus co-founded the **Music Rights Awareness Foundation (MRAF)** in 2016 with Max Martin and Niclas Molinder. In 2021 he launched **Credits Due** — an initiative to ensure complete, accurate song metadata is attached to all recordings at the point of creation. The problem it addresses: approximately **£500 million in earnings goes unallocated every year** due to missing or incomplete metadata.

This platform's automatic onboarding infrastructure (ISRC, ISWC, IPI, PRO registration, MLC registration) is the practical implementation of the Credits Due philosophy. Every artist onboarded correctly eliminates their contribution to that £500M black hole. This is a powerful alignment story — the platform is solving a problem that Björn Ulvaeus and Max Martin have been publicly fighting for years.

**Reference:** [Credits Due — MusicTech](https://musictech.com/news/abba-bjorn-ulvaeus-credits-due-music-royalty-500m/) | [Mixmag](https://mixmag.net/read/abbas-bjorn-program-music-royalties-news)

### Affordable Legal Path
Full-service securities attorneys are not the only option.

- **Volunteer Lawyers for the Arts (VLA)** — free or reduced-cost legal services specifically for artists and arts organizations, nationwide network
- **Law school IP / entrepreneurship clinics** — free, supervised by licensed attorneys
- **SCORE** — free mentorship from retired business executives for business structure questions
- **Startup attorneys on deferred fees** — some early-stage attorneys defer fees until funding or take a small equity stake
- **Clerky / Stripe Atlas / Doola** — LLC formation for $300–$500 flat, no attorney needed
- **SEC EDGAR public filings** — JKBX and SongVest filings are free to read and are structural blueprints before any attorney is engaged

## Revenue Collection Infrastructure — The Full Picture

Every dollar an artist earns needs to flow through the platform. Every transaction needs to be tracked, attributed, and publicly displayed in real time. That is the vision. What follows is an honest assessment of what that looks like in practice — what is achievable from day one, what requires building, and what requires the platform to become something bigger than a software product.

### The Core Challenge — Three Tiers of Feasibility

Not all revenue streams are equally capturable. The platform must be honest with itself about which tier each stream falls into and build toward total capture deliberately rather than promising it before it exists.

**Tier 1 — Fully in the system from day one**
These are transactions that originate inside the platform. The money never touches an outside system. Full real-time tracking and public display is achievable immediately.

| Revenue Stream | Why It's Capturable |
|---|---|
| Pledge raise transactions | Platform-native payment |
| Investment round purchases | Platform-native payment |
| Beat marketplace sales | Platform-native payment |
| Fan tips | Platform-native payment |
| Merch sold through platform store | Platform fulfillment, platform payment |
| POS app sales at shows | Artist uses platform app, Stripe Connect handles payment |
| Ad revenue distributions | Calculated and distributed internally |
| Influencer attribution earnings | Tracked and paid internally |

**Tier 2 — Capturable via API integration, with some delay**
These streams originate outside the platform but can be pulled in via authenticated connections. True real-time is not possible because the upstream source pays on its own schedule — but daily estimates from API data can be displayed with actuals reconciled when payments arrive.

| Revenue Stream | Delay | How It's Captured |
|---|---|---|
| Streaming earnings | Monthly payout, daily estimate | Distribution partner API (DistroKid/TuneCore) + direct Spotify for Artists, Apple Music for Artists connections |
| YouTube Content ID earnings | Monthly | YouTube Studio API via auth connection |
| PRO performance earnings | Quarterly | ASCAP/BMI portal connection |
| SoundExchange earnings | Quarterly | SoundExchange portal connection |
| MLC mechanical earnings | Monthly/quarterly | MLC portal connection |
| Bandcamp direct sales | Real-time via API | Bandcamp API via auth connection |
| Ticket sales (Eventbrite, DICE, etc.) | Near real-time | Ticketing platform API integration |

The dashboard shows these as two numbers simultaneously: **estimated** (updated daily from API data) and **confirmed** (updated when actual payments clear). Both are public. The difference between them is the pipeline — money that has been earned but not yet paid by the upstream source.

**Tier 3 — Requires the platform to become the contracting entity**
These streams are where the real integrity risk lives. The money currently goes directly to the artist or band — bypassing the system entirely. Capturing them requires the platform to insert itself as the party the outside world pays, then distribute inward.

| Revenue Stream | Current Risk | Required Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Venue performance guarantees | Venue pays artist directly in cash or ACH | Platform invoices the venue; venue pays the platform; platform distributes |
| Show door splits | Cash at the door, artist counts it | POS app handles all door transactions; venue registered in platform |
| Sync licensing fees | Deals made offline, fees paid to whoever negotiated | Platform is the sync licensing agent; all inquiries route through platform |
| Brand deal payments | Handshake deals, outside payment | Platform contracts with brands on artist's behalf above defined threshold |
| Session work fees | Outside invoices, personal payment | Required to be logged in platform if performed under the artist entity |

Tier 3 is the hardest and most important. It requires the platform to do something traditional software doesn't do: become the legal counterparty in real-world deals. The venue doesn't just connect to an API — it receives an invoice from SoundSteak LLC and pays SoundSteak LLC. This requires the platform to be a licensed money transmitter or to operate through a regulated payment partner who handles that status.

### The Money Movement Infrastructure

**Stripe Connect** is the most likely backbone for day one — it handles multi-party payments, marketplace splits, automatic distribution to multiple recipients, and has the regulatory framework for money transmission built in. It handles both platform-native transactions (Tier 1) and incoming payments from outside parties (Tier 3, once the platform is the contracting entity).

Every participating entity — artists, investors, professionals, promoters — has a Stripe Connect account linked to their platform profile. When money enters, it routes to the correct accounts automatically based on the documented ownership split. No human touches it. No one decides who gets what. The ownership table is the instruction set.

### What Real-Time Actually Means

The platform is not responsible for how fast an upstream source pays. Spotify pays monthly. PROs pay quarterly. That is their schedule and it cannot be changed. What the platform is responsible for is what happens the instant that money arrives.

The rule is simple: **the moment money hits the platform, it is live. Publicly. Immediately.**

No batching. No holding. No internal processing delay before the dashboard updates. The second a PRO payment clears, every SteakHolder with a stake in that recording sees the number change. The second a streaming payment arrives, the artist's dashboard updates and every investor's earnings reflect it in real time.

The platform does not control when upstream sources pay. It controls everything that happens after. That window — from receipt to public display to automatic distribution — is where the platform's commitment to transparency lives, and it must be as close to zero as technology allows.

**What the public dashboard shows for every artist:**
- **Incoming** — money received today, this week, this month, broken down by source
- **Distributed** — how that money was split and to whom, the moment it was processed
- **Pipeline** — earnings confirmed upstream but not yet received, with expected settlement dates pulled from each source's known payment schedule
- **Lifetime** — total earnings across every stream since signing, always current

Every number is public. Every source is labeled. The pipeline is not a hidden backstage number — it is displayed prominently, because investors watching an artist with $4,000 in confirmed pipeline earnings have information worth having.

Upstream payment timing is a fact of the industry. Hiding the gap between earned and received is a choice. SoundSteak does not make that choice.

### The Venue Problem — The Hardest Piece

Venues have been paying bands in cash at the end of the night since music venues existed. Getting that money into the system requires either:

**Option A — Platform becomes the contracting entity**
The platform books the show. The venue signs a contract with SoundSteak. The venue pays SoundSteak. SoundSteak distributes. This requires the platform to have a booking function and legal standing as a contracting party. Big lift — but the right long-term answer.

**Option B — Venue registration**
Venues register on the platform and agree to pay through the system as a condition of booking signed artists. Registered venues receive a preferred partner badge and visibility to every cohort of funded artists looking for show opportunities. The carrot: access to a pipeline of investment-backed artists with active fanbases. The stick: signed artists are contractually required to only accept show guarantees from registered venues or to log outside payments through the POS app.

**Option C — Artist logging with audit capability**
For unregistered venues, the artist logs the received payment through the POS app immediately after receiving it. The system records the amount, the date, the venue, and the artist's confirmation. Not as clean as Options A or B, but creates a documented record and establishes a paper trail. Gaps between reported show income and expected show income based on ticket sales data can be flagged automatically.

The path is Option C on day one, Option B as the venue network grows, Option A as the platform's scale and legal infrastructure supports it.

### Public Display — What Transparency Actually Looks Like

Every artist's earnings dashboard is public. Not just visible to investors — visible to anyone. This is radical transparency made concrete.

What the public sees on any artist's profile:
- Total lifetime earnings, broken down by stream
- Current month earnings, updating daily
- Pipeline earnings — confirmed but not yet paid
- Earnings per investor tier — what founding equity holders have received vs. product investors
- Every transaction above a defined threshold, timestamped and categorized
- The ownership table — exactly who holds what percentage and what they've received

This is not standard practice anywhere in the music industry. Labels guard this information. Artists often don't even know their own numbers. SoundSteak makes it the default and makes opacity the exception that requires justification.

## Revenue Integrity & Transaction Control

Every dollar an artist earns in their capacity as a signed SoundSteak artist flows through the system. No exceptions. This is not punitive — it is the deal. The band agreement signed at onboarding makes this explicit. The system protects artists from each other, protects investors from artists, and protects the platform from disputes.

### The Core Problem — The Gray Area
The most common integrity failure: an artist has a friend make shirts, sells them at shows, pockets the cash, and investors never see a penny of that revenue. This is not hypothetical — it is what bands do constantly. Left unresolved it destroys investor trust and makes the platform's ownership model meaningless.

### The Solution — Platform as Merch Provider
The platform is the default and required merch provider. Not optionally. Every unit is accounted for at the point of order — before the shirts leave the warehouse. Investors are covered before the product exists.

- Artist designs products inside the platform
- Orders at wholesale / platform cost price
- Receives inventory, sells at shows at their chosen price
- Margin between cost and sell price flows through the system and distributes automatically
- Pre-funded merch runs — investment raise includes a merch budget line; investors fund the first run, earnings distribute back as units sell

The band agreement explicitly prohibits unlicensed merch produced outside the platform for any signed project.

### The Point-of-Sale App
Every sale at every show must run through the system. The platform provides a dedicated **SoundSteak POS app** on the artist's phone. Every transaction — card or logged cash — runs through it. The app knows what was sold, at what price, calculates every stakeholder's cut in real time, and distributes automatically.

This is not a burden. It is easier than a personal Square account because the artist never thinks about splitting money afterward. It just happens.

The POS app is also a point-of-presence for the platform at every local show, every pop-up, every market. Anywhere the artist sells anything, the platform is there. That is reach no streaming deal provides.

### Revenue Stream Accountability

| Revenue Stream | Gray Area Risk | Platform Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Streaming | Low — tracked by ISRC | Direct API ingestion |
| Merch — platform store | None | Default fulfillment system |
| Merch — made outside platform | High | Band agreement prohibits unlicensed merch; platform is sole merch provider |
| Ticket sales | Medium | Platform POS app; integrated ticketing required |
| Live show guarantees | High — cash from venues | Venue must be platform-registered or payment logged through app |
| Sync licensing | Medium — deals made offline | All sync inquiries routed through platform; platform negotiates or approves |
| Brand deals | High — handshake agreements | Band agreement requires all deals above a threshold run through platform |
| YouTube AdSense | Low | Auth connection handles it |
| Fan tips / direct support | Medium — Venmo, cash | Platform provides tipping mechanism; external tips must be logged |
| Crowdfunding outside platform | High | Band agreement prohibits external crowdfunding for signed projects |
| Session work | Gray | Separate from signed artist revenue unless performed under artist entity |

### New Possibilities This Opens
- **Pre-funded merch** — investors fund the inventory run, artist sells it, earnings distribute back automatically
- **Show advance system** — platform advances touring costs against projected ticket earnings
- **Sync marketplace** — music supervisors browse platform catalog, licensing deals close inside the system, fees distribute automatically
- **Fan tipping** — a SoundSteak tip at a show goes through the system like any other transaction
- **Venue integration** — registered venues route show guarantee payments automatically
- **Wholesale purchasing** — artist buys merch at platform cost, sells at their own markup, margin distributes through system

## The Community Vote — Fan-Driven Collab Invitations

Every two weeks, the platform surfaces a new vote: **Who would you most want to see collab on a single?**

The community nominates and votes. The result is not a poll result — it is an invitation. SoundSteak publishes the outcome publicly and sends a formal invitation to the winning artist(s), signed by the platform and backed by the visible size and enthusiasm of the vote.

### Why This Works

The vote result is the pitch. An artist receiving an invitation already knows: a real audience actively wants this, campaigned for it, and is watching to see what happens. That is not a cold outreach. That is leverage.

The charity angle makes the invitation structurally difficult to decline. If the community has surfaced a cause the invited artist already publicly supports — through their own social platforms, their prior interviews, their known affiliations — the invitation becomes: "Your fans and [collab artist]'s fans are asking you to do something for a cause you already believe in." Declining that publicly is an awkward thing to do. Saying yes signals something real about the artist's character.

### How the Charity Works

There is no single designated charity per vote. The community can associate the invitation with any cause the artists involved are known to care about — and multiple charities can be named. An artist who cares about mental health access, and another who has spoken about environmental issues, can each have their cause acknowledged in the same invitation. The platform supports multiple charity designations per collab single. The percentage directed to each is agreed upon at signing and flows automatically through the distribution system, exactly like any other ownership split.

### The Full Participation Rule

This is foundational and non-negotiable: **every artist participating in a collab vote project participates in the platform fully.** There is no guest track path. No honorary credit. No lend-your-name-and-leave arrangement.

An established artist who says yes to a collab invitation joins the cycle. They go through the LLC formation, the full contract, the full investment structure, the milestone journey, the year of transparency. Their earnings are public. Their splits are documented. They are a SoundSteak artist for that project.

This makes the invitation more meaningful, not less. The artist who accepts is making a real commitment — and they know it before they say yes. The invitation letter is explicit about what participation involves. An artist who says yes to that is making a statement about what they believe in, not just doing a charity cameo.

It also means the collab single carries the same investor structure, the same SteakHolder access, the same LLC ownership as every other release on the platform. An established artist's name on a properly structured release, with transparent earnings and an investor pool, is an enormous signal to the market.

### The Cadence

One vote every two weeks. Enough frequency to feel alive — the community always has an active vote, a result arriving soon, and an invitation being watched. Not so frequent that each vote loses its moment or the community's attention fragments.

Each vote has a two-week window for community nominations and voting, followed by a public result announcement and the formal invitation. The invitation has a defined response window. If the artist declines or doesn't respond, the result is published transparently and the next vote opens. No outcome is hidden — the community that voted deserves to know what their vote produced.

### The Result Is Always Content

Whether the artist says yes or no, the vote result is a story:
- "15,000 people voted for this. Here's the letter we sent." — Published.
- An artist accepts — an extraordinary story about what the community made happen.
- An artist declines gracefully — published, and a signal that at least they engaged.
- No response — published, with the community's vote on record.

The transparency of the attempt is itself valuable. An artist who watches a vote they weren't part of, sees the community enthusiasm, and thinks "I'd want to be in the next one" — that is an acquisition mechanism no ad budget produces.

Every one of those companies employs full engineering teams focused exclusively on their one function. SoundSteak needs all of them working together, talking to each other, feeding a single unified dashboard, and distributing through a single payment infrastructure.

That is a genuinely enormous undertaking. There is no point pretending otherwise.

### The Strategy — Integration Layer First, Proprietary Infrastructure Over Time

The platform cannot be built from scratch all at once. Attempting to do so would take years and tens of millions of dollars before a single artist is signed. That is not the path.

**The day one architecture is an integration layer** — a unified interface that connects existing best-in-class services via their APIs and presents everything through a single dashboard. SoundSteak does not replace DistroKid on day one. It connects to DistroKid and pulls its data into the platform. It does not replace Stripe on day one — it uses Stripe Connect as the payment backbone. It does not replace Eventbrite — it integrates with Eventbrite's API for ticketing data.

The platform's proprietary value on day one is:
- The unified dashboard no other service provides
- The cap table and ownership management layer connecting everything
- The automatic distribution logic that splits every incoming payment correctly
- The governance and voting system — no existing solution does what this needs
- The artist journey management and automated marketing layer
- The conversational voice interface that no existing platform has

Everything else is an API call to an existing service.

**Proprietary infrastructure is built progressively** as volume justifies replacing a third-party service with an in-house equivalent. This mirrors the vertical integration roadmap — preferred partner first, equity stake later, ownership eventually. The same logic applies to software: use the existing service first, build the replacement when the platform is large enough to justify it.

## The Investment Model — Structure and Terms

### The Application Process

Applying to SoundSteak is not a form submission. It is a proof-of-existence process with two required elements:

**1. A verified live video.** The platform provides a recording mechanism — a real-time, platform-verified capture. Think Zoom-equivalent functionality built into the application flow. The system confirms the video is being recorded live, not uploaded from existing footage. Artists can optionally submit a higher-quality audio capture after the fact (self-recorded during the same session), but the platform-verified live video is the non-negotiable requirement. This is how SoundSteak confirms real artists with real music exist behind every application.

**2. Fan pledges.** At $1 each. The pledges cover pre-signing platform costs and prove that real people believe in the artist — not just the artist themselves. Hitting pledge goals requires building or activating a real audience, however small. Someone who has never played a show and has no community cannot hit them. That is the filter.

Pledge goals are tiered. The number and meaning of each goal will be worked out backward from what the platform's actual pre-signing costs are. Goal 1 is currently 50 pledges. Further goals and what they unlock or signal are to be determined as the cost model becomes clearer.

The pledge process also serves as onboarding: every person who pledges $1 creates a profile. The application is a growth mechanism, not just a selection mechanism. Every artist's campaign brings their network onto the platform before a contract has been signed.

The selection decision — who gets to the event and receives a contract — is fan-driven and data-driven. The label does not pick winners.

### The Two-Event Structure

The artist's journey from contract to release spans exactly two annual events. This is not a timeline — it is the architecture.

**Event One — The Contract and the IPO**
When the gong rings to open the show, two things happen simultaneously:
1. The artist is awarded their recording contract on stage
2. The single's ownership goes up for sale — the IPO is live

Investing begins the night the contract is signed. Not the night the single drops. That distinction is fundamental: investors fund the recording before it exists. They are backing the artist and the journey, not the finished product.

An LLC is formed for each single release at the point of signing. Investors are purchasing ownership in that LLC — a legal entity, not a token.

**Event Two — The Release**
Exactly one year later, at the next annual event, the artist performs their finished single for the first time. This is the graduation. Investors who backed the recording hear it live. The single is released to all platforms.

The funding threshold must be met within a defined window after Event One for recording to begin. Speed is by design. The platform does not sit on an open raise for months. If the threshold is not met in time, recording doesn't start and investors are returned their pledges. This is not a risk — it is a transparency mechanism. A raise that fills fast is itself a signal to the market.

### LLC Per Release

At signing, an LLC is formed for that single. This entity:
- Owns the recording
- Receives all earnings from that recording
- Has an investor cap table as its ownership structure
- Files taxes and issues 1099s automatically through the platform
- Has a pre-defined exit (buyout) clause written into its operating agreement

The platform handles all LLC formation. The artist does not navigate this alone. One of the concrete benefits of the SoundSteak path versus going independent is that the administrative infrastructure of being a musician is handled by default.

### Ownership Structure

**Platform fee** comes off the top first. This is non-negotiable — it funds operations and is disclosed in full before any investment is made. The artist's percentage and investor percentage are calculated on what remains after the platform fee.

**Artist ownership**: The artist decides how much of their share to retain. They can keep any percentage they choose — or give away all of it if that is their decision. They can also designate a portion of their share to a charity of their choice. That is documented in the contract and flows automatically through the distribution system.

**Investor ownership**: The remaining stake — whatever the artist has not retained — is the pool available for investment.

**The alignment principle**: This record label does what is in the best interest of the artist — because that is what is in the best interest of the SteakHolders, and in turn, SoundSteak. That alignment is not a policy. It is the structure.

### Pre-Orders — The Merch Machine Starts at Signing

Pre-orders for merchandise (and anything else the platform can offer) begin immediately after the contract is signed — before a shirt is designed, before anything is printed. The sequence is:

1. Contract signed at Event One
2. Artist designs the first piece of merch on the platform
3. Pre-orders go live on the artist's profile immediately — before production
4. SteakHolders pre-purchase and share the listing
5. When enough pre-orders exist to justify a print run, the run happens — zero inventory risk

Pre-orders are not tied to the single's release date. They are tied to the signing. This means pre-orders launch, run, and fulfill on their own timeline independent of when the music drops.

**Why this matters for delayed artists:** If an artist slips their release to a later event, the merch machine keeps running. New orders continue to open. The community's ongoing financial activity — buying, sharing, pre-ordering — keeps the relationship alive through the delay. A setback communicated transparently, with visible community support and active pre-orders, can actually deepen SteakHolder investment rather than erode it. The pre-order becomes a public vote of confidence. The delay becomes a chapter in the story, not the end of it.

**The fan as the marketing machine:** Every SteakHolder who shares a pre-order link is acting in their own financial interest. They don't need to be asked. The economic alignment turns passive supporters into an active, self-motivated distribution network — functioning simultaneously as manager, street team, PR firm, and marketing agency, all running on the same engine: their own stake in the outcome.

### Ongoing Investment — No Rounds

There is no founding round, no Series A, no secondary round. Investment in a release is a continuous, open opportunity for as long as the music is on the platform. The LLC earns indefinitely. New revenue streams open as the platform grows — sync placements years after release, new markets, new platforms, new opportunities the team hasn't anticipated yet. The platform's standing mandate is to find every possible way to maximize what a stake earns and to keep improving that number over time.

"Founding round" language is specifically avoided. It implies the window closes. This window doesn't close.

### First Come, First Served

No minimums. No maximums. One person can buy the entire available stake if they are fast enough to press the button. This is not a rule for the sake of simplicity — it is a philosophical statement about access. The fan who has $10 and acts in the first minute has the same shot as the investor with $10,000 who acts in the second minute.

This creates urgency. It rewards attention and conviction. It makes the IPO night feel like what it is: a once-in-a-year moment where early believers get in first.

### The Funding Threshold

A minimum funding goal is set before each raise opens. This represents the actual cost of recording the single — studio time, production, mixing, mastering, video, PR. Every line item is publicly disclosed. The platform knows the cost of everything because it has to — the funding goal must be defensible to every investor who reads it.

If the threshold is not met within the defined window: the project does not proceed. Investors receive full refunds. The artist is not penalized — they reapply for the next cycle. Threshold failure before recording begins protects everyone. It is far less disruptive than a project stalling mid-production.

### The Buyout

Every band agreement and every investor disclosure includes a clear, pre-defined buyout clause. Investors must know from the moment they invest that a buyout is a possible future event — not a surprise exit, but a transparent mechanism.

A buyout means a third party (a label, the artist, or any other buyer) purchases all outstanding SteakHolder ownership in the LLC at a defined formula. Investors receive their buyout payment. The LLC's ownership is consolidated under the buyer. The music moves on.

Investors should understand the buyout not as a risk but as a feature: it is one of the potential exit paths in an otherwise illiquid investment. A buyout at a premium is a return, not a loss.

### Terminology — Internal vs. Public

**"Cycle"** is the public-facing term for each cohort of artists from one annual event to the next. "Apply for the next cycle." "The Class of 2027."

**"Cohort"** is the internal operational term. Used in team communications, this document, and platform back-end systems. Never used in marketing, landing pages, or public communications.

### Event Capacity as the Natural Scale Governor

The number of performers per event is capped at five — set by the stage, not by a business decision. But the number of artists signed in a given cycle is not the same as the number who perform. All signed artists attend Event One. Only the top five (ranked by pledge support) take the stage. The signing class can grow as operations allow without changing the event experience.

This separates two things that are easy to conflate: **the signing class** (can scale) and **the performance slots** (always five). Investors get more deal flow as the platform grows. The event never gets diluted. Both things are true at once.

Scale comes from growing the signing class and, eventually, adding more events per year in other cities — not from putting fifteen artists on one stage. A second annual event in a second city doubles the capacity without changing what either night feels like.

This is not a workaround for logistics — it is a philosophical choice. The scarcity of a performance slot is part of what makes performing matter. The signing class can expand; the stage stays intimate.

### The Honest Recommendation — Start With Three
The first cycle sets the standard everything is judged against. Three artists with an extraordinary, fully supported experience and documented results is a stronger foundation than five artists with a diluted one.

**Cycle 1 — Three artists. Prove the model. Build the playbook.**
**Cycle 2 — Five artists. Scale with confidence.**
**Cycle 3+ — Expand signing class as team, systems, and event capacity allow. Add a second event before overfilling the first.**

The event still works beautifully with three artists. Three IPOs on one night, one gong ring, one extraordinary evening. The intimacy of three might actually be more powerful than five for the inaugural event.

## Edge Cases & Contingency Planning

The platform will encounter situations no standard record deal anticipated. These need answers before they happen — because the first time any of these lands, investors are watching how the platform handles it. The response to a crisis is itself a statement of values.

---

### Release Timeline — Delays, Slippage, and the Three-Event Rule

**The standard timeline:** Event One (signing + IPO) → one year → Event Two (single release). This is the goal. Everything on the platform is designed to make it achievable: milestone-gated capital, platform-managed logistics, Central Intelligence monitoring, pre-orders and community activity keeping momentum alive.

**Timeline slippage.** Life happens. A delayed artist who misses their Event Two release does not lose their moment — they carry it forward to the next annual event. The same full showcase treatment applies regardless of when it arrives: same stage, same celebration, no lesser moment for being late. The story of an artist who had a hard year and showed up anyway is worth telling. Investors are notified of the delay promptly and honestly. Pre-orders remain open and new orders continue — the ongoing commercial activity around the artist is one of the primary mechanisms for sustaining investor confidence through a delay.

**The three-event rule.** An artist who misses three consecutive annual events without releasing their single is removed from the platform. Three years is generous. Three years with active platform support, pre-orders running, a SteakHolder community intact, and milestone-gated production infrastructure available is enough time. If it hasn't happened in three years, it is not going to happen.

At the three-event threshold, the exit financial process engages automatically:
- The artist LLC is formally closed
- Any remaining undepleted capital in escrow is returned to investors proportionally
- Deployed capital that was spent on verifiable production work (recorded sessions, paid producers, filed registrations) is documented and disclosed — investors understand what their money built even if the final product didn't materialize
- The artist's founding equity stake, held in escrow at signing, is liquidated to cover any breach-related obligations
- Investors receive a full transparent accounting of what was deployed, what was recovered, and what was lost

**Artist in the top five can't perform at Event One.** The next artist in the signing class by pledge ranking takes the slot. The stage is never empty. All signed artists are present in the room regardless — the non-performing artist still receives their contract, their IPO opens on event night, and they are part of the class. No special ceremony is needed. The slot goes to someone ready to fill it.

**Artist can't perform at Event Two but the single is finished.** The music video plays at the event instead. This is a real option and not a lesser one — a premiere on the event stage for a room full of SteakHolders is a moment in its own right. The single releases on schedule. Earnings begin immediately. The artist addresses the room however they can.

**Artist can't record the single at all.** Two distinct scenarios:

*Threshold never met:* Funding goal didn't clear within the defined window. Minimal capital was deployed (administrative costs only). Pledges are returned. The artist can reapply in a future cycle. Clean and fast.

*Threshold met, capital deployed, recording didn't happen:* The milestone system limits the exposure — capital only releases against verified progress. The maximum investor loss is bounded by what was already released and spent on documented production work. This risk is disclosed in the offering documents at the point of investment. "Investors knew the risks" is not callous — it is what securities law requires, and it is honest. What distinguishes compassionate handling from breach enforcement is the question of whether the artist tried and circumstances prevented completion, or whether they simply didn't perform. The contract's breach clause, the artist's escrowed founding equity, and the platform's documented record of what happened are the instruments of resolution.

### Artist-Side Edge Cases

**The artist goes completely off-grid mid-journey.**
Life happens. A religious conversion, a mental health crisis, a family emergency — whatever the cause, the artist stops engaging entirely. Not a slow fade. A full stop.

*Response:* The band agreement at signing defines a non-performance clause. A defined window of non-responsiveness — 30 or 60 days — triggers a formal notice process. Three documented attempts at contact (platform, email, phone) with no response initiates the exit protocol. Investors are notified transparently. The artist's LLC is placed in a holding state. Earnings already distributed stay distributed. Future earnings are held in escrow pending resolution. The artist can re-engage within a defined grace period and resume. After that period, the platform exercises its exit rights. The label's percentage of creative ownership — documented in the original contract — allows it to pursue completion of contracted deliverables through alternative means, subject to what the contract permits. The exit protocol is not punitive — it is a pre-agreed answer to a pre-agreed question.

**The artist goes Amish.** *(Or equivalent — any sincere rejection of technology and the modern economy.)*
A genuine philosophical or religious withdrawal from commerce. Not a legal fight — a personal one. They are not attempting to harm anyone. They simply want out.

*Response:* This is handled as the voluntary exit scenario with one addition: sincerity matters. A documented pattern of genuine withdrawal — not just a negotiating tactic — should be treated with grace. If the artist has genuinely left the building, forcing them to complete a recording contract serves nobody. The platform should have a compassionate early exit path that returns creative ownership to the artist, returns investor capital from escrow if the project never launched, and closes cleanly. No punitive damages for sincere departure before significant investment is deployed. The contract language should anticipate this. The integrity of the platform's relationship with artists is worth more than enforcing one contract against someone who genuinely doesn't want to be there.

**The artist is offered a major label contract and attempts to exit.**
The dream scenario for the artist — and the nightmare scenario for investors. A major label wants them, they want out, and they may hire an attorney to make that happen.

*Response:* The contract is clear. They signed a single contract for a specific product. They cannot un-sign it. The recording they agreed to make belongs to the LLC. They can sign with a major label for future projects — the platform's contract covers one song, not their soul. If the label wants the song too, the platform negotiates a buyout on behalf of investors at a premium. A buyout at above-par value is actually a win for early investors. If the artist refuses to complete the recording, they are in breach. The breach clause specifies damages, which are funded by their founding equity stake held in escrow. The platform's legal exposure here is manageable if the contract is properly drafted from day one. This scenario is an argument for rock-solid single-contract language above everything else.

**The artist tries to legally challenge the contract itself.**
Not an exit request — active legal action claiming the contract is unconscionable, that they didn't understand what they signed, or that the platform's securities structure is invalid.

*Response:* This is the most serious artist-side scenario. Defenses: clear and plain-language contracts reviewed by music attorney before any artist signs, mandatory independent legal review period built into the signing process (artist given 10 days and encouraged to seek counsel), all contracts publicly filed. The best protection is an extremely clean, fair, and plainly-worded contract that no attorney can credibly call exploitative. The platform's artist-first philosophy is not just values — it is a legal strategy. It is very hard to argue exploitation when the contract is shorter, clearer, and more artist-friendly than anything the major label system offers.

**The artist passes away before the showcase, during the journey, or before the release.**
Death mid-journey. Investors are in, production may be underway, the single may be partially or fully recorded.

*Response:* Three distinct timing scenarios, three distinct responses.

*Before signing:* Straightforward. The spot goes to the next artist in the selection process. No investors are involved yet.

*After signing, before the investment raise fully deploys:* Pledge round investors are notified immediately. The platform offers full refund of pledges from platform operational reserves. The LLC is dissolved. The artist's estate is treated respectfully and with transparency.

*After production is substantially complete — recording done or nearly done:* This is the most complex case. The estate inherits the artist's ownership stake and the creative rights under the estate's control. The band agreement governs what happens to other members' stakes. If the project can be completed and released — and the estate and surviving members consent — the platform may proceed with release as a memorial project. Earnings flow to the estate per the documented splits. Investors are given the option to exit at par or remain invested in the completed project. No decision is forced. Full transparency throughout.

A death clause must be in every band agreement. It is not morbid — it is responsible.

**The artist is incapacitated — serious illness, injury, accident.**
Not a permanent exit but an indefinite pause. The artist wants to continue but cannot right now.

*Response:* The journey timeline is paused, not ended. Investors are notified of the delay with honest framing. A hold period is defined. If the artist recovers and re-engages, the journey resumes from where it paused. If recovery is unlikely or the delay extends past a defined maximum, the artist and platform agree on resolution — completion by other means, graceful exit, or in rare cases a hardship release. The platform absorbs some cost here because it is the right thing to do and because how it handles this will be watched by every other artist considering the platform.

**The artist is accused of serious misconduct — criminal charges, hate speech, abuse allegations.**
The artist is now a reputational liability. Investors may demand action. Other artists in the cohort may be affected.

*Response:* The platform is not a court. It does not convict or punish based on allegations. But it does have obligations to investors, to other artists, and to its own integrity. The platform's code of conduct — agreed to at signing — includes a conduct clause. Documented, credible, serious misconduct activates a review process with a defined timeline. During review, the artist's marketing support is paused. Earnings continue to flow because investors own those earnings and the platform cannot withhold them without investor consent. If the platform determines the conduct clause was violated, it exercises the right to terminate the marketing and support relationship while the financial obligations to investors continue. The artist's stake is not confiscated — that would be punitive without due process — but the platform's active support ends. Investors are given the option to exit at the current valuation.

Allegations that prove false: the platform resumes full support with a public acknowledgment that the review process cleared the artist.

**A band member leaves or is fired — the full matrix.**

This is the most common band scenario in the history of music and the one the platform must handle with the most precision. Timing changes everything. The platform's individual payment architecture means earnings protection is already built in — but the contract language must address every variation.

*The core principle that never changes:* A member cannot be stripped of earnings from work they already contributed to. Ever. The platform enforces this mechanically — it does not require good behavior from the remaining members to make it happen. Their cut of a finished recording flows to their individual profile permanently. The band cannot vote it away, withhold it, or redirect it. This is non-negotiable.

**Scenario A — A member leaves or is fired before recording begins.**
The cleanest exit. Nothing has been produced. The investment raise may or may not be funded.

If the raise is not yet funded: the departing member's founding equity stake from the pledge round is governed by a vesting schedule in the band agreement. Early departure before a defined milestone may result in partial or full forfeiture of that stake — this protects the remaining members and investors from someone collecting equity for work they did not do. The raise continues with the remaining lineup. Investors are notified of the lineup change. If the change is material — the lead singer leaves before a single note is recorded — investors may be offered the option to exit their pledge at par.

If the raise is fully funded and recording is imminent: same vesting logic applies to the equity stake. The recording budget is held pending a decision — does the band continue with a replacement member, or does the project close? The band agreement must specify the minimum viable lineup for the project to proceed. If below that threshold, investors are offered exit at par and the project is paused or closed.

**Scenario B — A member leaves or is fired during recording.**
The complicated middle. Some tracks may feature the departing member, some may not yet be started.

The departing member retains a documented credit — and earnings stake — for every track they performed on. Their contribution is logged in the platform the moment the session happens. What they recorded, when, their agreed split for those contributions. This is immutable. The remaining members cannot retroactively remove someone from a track they played on.

For unrecorded portions: the band agreement specifies how the departing member's originally agreed overall split is recalculated for the final delivered work. If they played on 60% of the recording, they may retain 60% of their original stake — or a negotiated equivalent. This requires legal language worked out in advance, not improvised under emotional pressure after someone is fired.

A replacement member may be brought in for the remaining recording. Their stake is negotiated between them and the band and documented on the platform before their first session. They have no claim to tracks recorded before they joined.

**Scenario C — A member leaves or is fired after recording is complete, before release.**
The record is done. Someone quit. Or was pushed out.

The recording is a finished asset. The LLC exists. The departing member's stake in that recording is documented, permanent, and enforced by the platform. They will receive their earnings forever. Full stop. The remaining members cannot block this, vote it away, or withhold it through any mechanism the platform controls.

What they cannot take with them: the right to block the release. The band agreement must explicitly state that no single member holds veto power over the release of a completed and investor-funded recording. If they attempt to block release through legal means, they are interfering with investor interests — which changes the legal character of the dispute significantly.

**Scenario D — A member leaves or is fired after release, while the recording is actively earning.**
The simplest scenario financially — the platform already handles this automatically. Earnings continue flowing to their individual profile indefinitely. No action required by anyone. The band cannot redirect their cut. The system does not ask the band for permission on every payment.

The complexity here is human, not financial: the departing member may be bitter, may speak publicly about the band, may create reputational damage. The platform does not adjudicate personal conflicts. It continues paying everyone correctly and lets the humans sort out the rest.

**Scenario E — A firing is disputed. One person says they quit. The other says they were fired.**
"He quit." "No, they fired me."

This is a dispute between humans that the platform cannot resolve. What the platform can do: log every formal communication through the platform's internal messaging system so there is a timestamped record of what was said and when. The band agreement should define what constitutes a legal departure — written notice through the platform, not a text message argument. Verbal firings and rage-quits with no documentation create legal ambiguity. The contract establishes the only legally recognized departure mechanism.

Until the dispute is resolved — between the parties, with mediators, or in court — the departing member's earnings are held in escrow. The platform does not take sides and does not release disputed funds to either party without documentation of resolution.

**Scenario F — The founding member is pushed out by the rest of the band.**
The person who started the band and brought them to the platform — potentially the person who drove the showcase application — is voted out by the other members.

This is the most emotionally and legally fraught variation. If the founding member holds a disproportionate equity stake by virtue of founding the project, the remaining members may be trying to force them out to capture more of the economics. This is attempted in bands constantly. The platform does not allow it.

The founding member's equity is documented and immutable. They cannot be financially pushed out of their documented stake. They can be removed from the band as a creative and performing unit — that is between the humans. They cannot be removed from their earnings. The band agreement must additionally address: what happens to the band name? If the founding member's name or identity is the band identity, the remaining members may not be able to continue using it. This is a separate legal question from platform economics and must be resolved by the parties.

**Scenario G — A replacement member joins. What rights do they have?**
Someone is brought in to replace a departed member. The project is mid-journey.

Replacement members negotiate their stake directly with the remaining band members. The platform facilitates the documentation of that agreement — it does not set the terms. Once agreed and documented on the platform, the replacement member's stake is as immutable as any original member's. They are eligible for earnings on any track they contribute to from the point they join. They have no retroactive claim on tracks recorded before they arrived.

If the replacement member's contribution is significant — they re-record parts, they co-write additional material, they bring a measurable audience — their stake should reflect that. If they are purely a live touring addition with no creative contribution to the recorded single, their stake may be zero on the recorded project and negotiated separately for live earnings.

**Scenario H — Enough members leave that the band is no longer the band.**
Three members of a five-piece quit. Two remain. Are they still the band?

The band agreement must define a minimum viable lineup threshold. Below that threshold the project enters a hold state. The remaining members have a defined window to either recruit replacements to above the threshold or agree to close the project. Investor funds not yet deployed are returned. Completed work's earnings continue flowing to all documented contributors.

The platform does not force a band to continue. It also does not allow two people to continue collecting investment money under a band name that no longer meaningfully represents that band to the investors who backed it.

**Scenario I — A fired member goes public and actively damages the band.**
They post accusations, share private material, run a campaign to hurt the remaining members' reputation and streaming numbers.

The platform cannot control what happens off-platform. It can document its own records clearly — timestamped communications, documented disputes, formal departure notices — which may be relevant if the situation escalates legally. The remaining band and the platform both have interests in protecting investor value. If the former member's actions constitute tortious interference with a business relationship, that is a legal matter between the parties. The platform cooperates with any legal process and provides its documented records.

**Scenario J — A fired member claims they wrote more of the songs than was documented.**
After they're out, they argue their songwriting contribution entitles them to a larger share of earnings.

Songwriting splits are registered with the PRO at the point of release — not at the point of dispute. The platform requires all splits to be agreed, documented, and submitted before the investment raise opens. This creates a timestamped, pre-dispute record of what was agreed. If a member signs off on the splits at signing and then later disputes them, the PRO's records and the platform's documentation are the evidence. This is not a new kind of dispute — PROs have dispute resolution processes that are the industry standard for exactly this. The platform follows those processes and submits its documentation to support them.

**The artist defrauds investors — fakes streaming numbers, buys followers, manufactures metrics.**
The pitch to investors was based on inflated data. The artist deliberately manipulated the numbers the platform presented.

*Response:* Platform-ingested data comes directly from authenticated connections to official APIs. Spotify for Artists data is pulled directly from Spotify — it cannot be inflated by the artist on the platform's dashboard. The platform is therefore somewhat insulated from metric fraud by design. However, follower fraud on social platforms is harder to detect. The platform's verification layer should include engagement rate analysis, not just follower counts — purchased followers do not engage. A verified artist with 50K followers and 0.1% engagement is flagged by the system for review before any raise is opened. If fraud is discovered post-investment, it is securities fraud — a federal matter. The platform cooperates fully with any investigation and has the documented data to show it was also a victim of the fraud, not a participant.

**An investor dies.**
A SteakHolder passes away. Their stake needs to go somewhere.

*Response:* The stake is a financial asset. It passes through the estate like any other investment account. The platform works with the estate's executor to transfer the stake to the beneficiary. This requires a secondary transfer mechanism — the technical system must support stake transfer between individuals, not just initial purchase. The earnings continue to flow into escrow during the transfer process and are released to the new owner when the transfer is complete. This is documented in the investor disclosure materials at the point of purchase.

**An investor wants to exit and there is no secondary market yet.**
The investor needs their money back. The platform doesn't have a secondary market built. They are stuck.

*Response:* Investor disclosure at the point of purchase must clearly state that this investment may be illiquid for a defined period. No secondary market = no guaranteed exit. This is standard securities disclosure language and the investor agreed to it. However, the platform should build at least a basic peer-to-peer stake transfer mechanism early — not a full exchange, but a way for investors to offer their stake to other platform members. A willing seller, a willing buyer, and a platform-facilitated transfer at an agreed price. This is simpler to build and to regulate than a full secondary market and handles the majority of liquidity requests. The full secondary market comes later.

### Platform-Side Edge Cases

**Another company sues the platform — general litigation risk.**
A competitor claims the platform infringed their patent, their trademark, their trade secret, or their business model. A former employee sues. A vendor sues over a contract dispute. A disgruntled artist files a civil claim.

*Response:* Litigation is a cost of operating a platform. It cannot be prevented — it can only be managed. Key preparations:
- Proper corporate structure from day one — the platform entity is the defendant, not the founder personally
- D&O insurance (Directors and Officers liability) before anything of significance happens
- E&O insurance (Errors and Omissions) for platform operations
- IP audit before launch — the platform name, brand, and any patentable processes should be clear of existing claims before the business is public
- Clean contracts with every vendor, partner, and employee — no handshake deals
- Legal counsel on retainer by the time money is moving
The platform cannot afford to fight a well-funded opponent in prolonged litigation. The best defense is a clean operation, properly documented, with no obvious legal vulnerabilities for a plaintiff to exploit.

**A major streaming platform changes its API or cuts off third-party access.**
Spotify, Apple Music, or another platform revokes developer access or changes terms in a way that breaks the platform's data ingestion.

*Response:* No single integration dependency should be a single point of failure. The platform builds abstraction layers around each integration so that if one breaks, others still function and the broken one can be repaired or replaced without taking down the dashboard. Distribution is handled through licensed aggregator relationships (DistroKid, TuneCore) rather than direct platform API — those relationships are more stable than API access. Investor-facing data should always show a "last updated" timestamp so that a delay in data is visible and explainable rather than silently wrong.

**The platform runs out of money mid-cohort.**
Operational funds are exhausted. Artists are mid-journey. Investors are in.

*Response:* This is the scenario every investor fears and the platform must be designed to make survivable. Key protections:
- Artist investment raise funds are held in the artist LLC — they are not the platform's operating capital. The platform cannot spend them on operations. They are legally separated.
- Platform revenue (fees and percentages) funds operations. If that runs dry, the platform can reduce operations without touching investor or artist funds.
- A minimum operating reserve threshold — below which the platform triggers a restructuring process before it hits zero
- Investor notification at defined thresholds: "Platform has 6 months of operational runway" is a fact investors deserve to know
- A platform emergency fund — a percentage of all fees held in a reserve specifically for continuity in a cash crunch
If the platform must close, the wind-down process must protect investors first. The LLCs continue to exist and earn independently of the platform's operational status. Earnings continue to route. The platform closing is painful — but it should not be able to take investor money with it.

**A data breach — user data, financial data, or platform systems are compromised.**
The platform is hacked. Personal data, payment information, and potentially financial stake data is exposed.

*Response:* As a platform handling securities transactions, a data breach has regulatory notification requirements, not just ethical ones. The plan: SOC 2 compliance as a design goal from the beginning, not a retrofit. No storing of payment credentials — payment handled by a regulated partner (Stripe Connect). OAuth tokens rotated regularly. Breach response plan documented before launch: who is notified, in what order, in what timeframe. Artist and investor data is the platform's most sensitive asset. It must be treated accordingly from day one.

**The annual event cannot happen — natural disaster, pandemic, venue failure, permit denial.**
The showcase is canceled or must be moved weeks or days out.

*Response:* The event exists in two forms — physical and livestream. A physical cancellation is survivable if the livestream continues. The entire selection and voting process is already online. The signing and pledge raise activation can happen digitally. A year-one contingency plan should include a fully functional virtual version that can be activated on short notice. Artists still get their contracts. Raises still go live. The gong still rings — on camera, from wherever the artists are. Disappointing for Year 1, but not fatal. The event's cultural significance is in what happens, not where it happens.

**A competitor directly copies the platform model.**
A well-funded company or existing music platform builds something indistinguishable from SoundSteak.

*Response:* Documented in the Protecting the Idea section. What cannot be copied fast: the network, the event track record, the established cohort history, the artist trust built over years, and the Central Intelligence agent's accumulated operational knowledge. A copycat can replicate the idea. They cannot replicate the reality of it working. The answer to this threat is the same as the answer to most threats: move, ship, execute.

**The SEC or a state securities regulator challenges the platform's structure.**
A regulatory authority determines that the platform's offering does not qualify under Reg A+ or raises concerns about the $1 pledge structure.

*Response:* The legal structure is designed around precedent — SongVest, JKBX, and Royalty Flow were all reviewed and qualified by the SEC. A challenge is unlikely if the structure mirrors theirs closely. However: no significant money moves until a securities attorney has reviewed and signed off. The SEC EDGAR filings of precedent companies are the starting point. Deviation from those precedents requires deliberate justification. If a regulator raises a concern, the platform has documented its reasoning and its precedents, cooperates fully with the inquiry, and adjusts where required. Do not fight the SEC. Work with them.

**A songwriter co-writer dispute emerges after a recording is released.**
Someone who was not part of the onboarding process comes forward claiming they co-wrote part of a song that is now generating investor returns.

*Response:* This is a music industry problem older than the recording industry itself. The platform's protections: song registration at the PRO includes all claimed co-writers at the point of registration. The band agreement requires every member to declare all co-write arrangements before the investment raise opens. If a co-writer claim emerges after the fact, the disputed portion of earnings is held in escrow while the dispute is resolved through the PRO's standard process. The platform is not the arbiter — the PRO is. The platform implements the PRO's resolution. Investors are notified of the hold and the reason. The platform's liability is limited because it acted on the information provided at signing.

**An investor attempts to manipulate Cues — coordinated bloc voting, buying votes, coercion.**
A large investor or coordinated group attempts to use voting power to harm an artist, extract unfair value, or sabotage a competitor's investment.

*Response:* Voting power is proportional to stake — that is fair. Coordination is not inherently improper. But coercion, harassment, and vote manipulation that constitutes market manipulation under securities law is illegal, not just a platform policy violation. The platform monitors for unusual voting patterns — a bloc of small new accounts all voting identically on a single issue is a signal worth reviewing. Artists retain creative veto. Governance scope is legally defined and cannot be stretched through vote. The platform's legal terms prohibit market manipulation and reserve the right to invalidate votes that demonstrate coordinated bad-faith participation.

## Protecting the Idea

### The Real Vulnerabilities
- **Ideas have no legal protection.** Copyright and patents don't cover concepts. If someone reads a description of this and builds it, there is no legal recourse. This has always been true.
- **The technical barrier is lower.** AI tools have made complex builds more accessible to anyone with time and a subscription. The complexity moat that once felt like protection is shrinking.
- **A well-funded competitor could move faster.** Capital and a development team could potentially outbuild to market if the concept became widely known before execution begins.
- **Chat interfaces carry privacy risk.** AI conversation history is not inherently private. Sensitive planning should happen in tools whose privacy model is understood and trusted.

### What Cannot Be Stolen
- **The contextual data catalog.** A competitor can scrape audio. They cannot scrape artist intent, compositional notes, influence maps, lyrical themes, and the documented year-long journey — because that data only exists if you have the artist relationship, the signing process, and the documentation pipeline. Every artist signed is a contextual data asset that deepens over time and cannot be reverse-engineered from the recording alone.
- **The network.** The show organizer, studio owners, bands, the Gamechanger Audio relationship — none of that transfers to a competitor. They would have to cold-call their way into an industry that runs entirely on trust. You already have the trust.
- **Insider credibility as a musician.** Artists will join a platform built by someone who has been in a band, understands the money disputes, and knows the frustration firsthand. That cannot be manufactured by a well-funded tech company.
- **First mover advantage in a relationship-driven industry.** The artists signed first, the sponsors landed first, the showcase run first — those become the case studies, the proof, the reputation. In music, loyalty runs deep. The first label that treats artists right earns a position that is almost impossible to displace.
- **Execution is still the hard part.** The code is more accessible now. The legal structure, the event production, the sponsor relationships, the artist trust, the regulatory compliance — none of that got easier. Most people who get excited about an idea still don't finish it.

### Practical Safeguards
- **Document everything with dates.** Every version of this document, every idea written down — timestamped records establish prior art even without formal legal protection. If a dispute ever arose, provable development history matters.
- **Lock down the name and brand early.** Once a name is chosen: register the LLC, claim the domain, file for trademark. An idea has no legal protection. A named, registered, operating company does.
- **Use NDAs for key conversations.** When detailed discussions begin with the show organizer, studio owners, or early partners — simple NDAs are free to generate and establish good-faith confidentiality expectations. Most people in the existing network won't need one, but the habit protects as conversations get more serious.
- **Move faster than feels comfortable.** The best protection against idea theft is execution. Every week nothing tangible exists is a week someone else could theoretically catch up. Legal steps, first sponsor conversations, first informal showcase discussions — these create facts on the ground that are much harder to replicate than a document.
- **Be deliberate about what is shared in cloud-connected tools.** Check privacy settings on any AI or cloud tool used for planning. The document on a local desktop is safer than chat history in a browser interface.

### The Bottom Line
The complexity moat was always an illusion — it felt real because execution was hard. What was actually providing protection was being the only one thinking about this seriously. That is still true. The person most likely to build this is still you — because you have spent years developing the vision, accumulating the relationships, and building the specific credibility that makes it work. AI leveled the playing field on code. It did not level the playing field on relationships, vision, or the willingness to actually finish something.

## The Voice Layer — Conversational Access for Every Participant

Every person on the platform — artist, investor, fan, band member, producer, studio owner — has a direct conversational line to the platform's intelligence. Not a help desk. Not a FAQ bot. Not a ticket system. A conversation, in their own words, from wherever they are.

The system already knows who they are, what they own, where they are in their journey, and what the data says about their situation. When they speak to it, it listens with full context. It does not ask them to navigate a menu to find the right department. It just asks: *what's going on?*

---

### Why This Is the Most Important Idea in the Platform

Every other feature in this document is about the platform watching and acting on data. This is about the platform listening to people.

Data tells the system what happened. People tell the system what is *about* to happen — and why.

An ISRC code returning lower-than-expected streaming revenue is a data anomaly. An artist saying *"something feels off — we played three shows last month and barely saw anything from the merch"* is a human signal. Both matter. The second one often arrives before the data catches up, and it contains context the data never could — tone, worry, suspicion, confusion. The conversational layer turns every participant into an active sensor for the platform's intelligence.

This is the difference between a monitoring system and a nervous system.

---

### Every Pocket Is a Window In

Everyone carries a phone. The platform's conversational interface lives in every one of them simultaneously.

A 19-year-old fan in rural Georgia who just pledged a dollar on a band she heard at a bar asks: *"what does owning a stake actually mean for me?"* She gets a plain answer calibrated to her level of familiarity — not a legal disclosure, not a link to the FAQ.

A 54-year-old serious investor evaluating a portfolio asks: *"how is the Class of 2027 cohort performing against last year's class at this stage?"* He gets a data-dense comparison because the system knows who he is and what he wants.

A bass player who just got fired texts: *"they just kicked me out — do I lose my money?"* He gets an immediate, honest, specific answer about his documented earnings stake — not a form to fill out.

A band leader asks: *"our drummer has been MIA for two weeks — what does that mean for our timeline?"* The system cross-references the cohort calendar, the current milestone stage, and the band agreement, and tells them exactly what the window is before formal notice procedures begin.

Same platform. Four completely different conversations. All of them useful. None of them possible through a dashboard alone.

---

### The Multi-Perspective Picture

The most powerful thing the conversational layer does is hear from every side simultaneously — in their own words, unprompted, from their own pocket.

The band leader says: *"we had to let our guitarist go — it wasn't working out."*

The guitarist says: *"I got fired out of nowhere, no warning, the day before we were supposed to start recording."*

The investor asks: *"I heard there might be some lineup drama with this band — should I be worried?"*

The system holds all three of these. It does not take sides. It does not reveal what one party said to another. But it now has a complete picture that no single data stream could provide. It knows this is a disputed departure, that the timing is sensitive, that at least one investor is already anxious. It can flag this for human review with the full context assembled — not just "band member departure detected" but the texture of what happened, from every angle, in the words of the people who lived it.

This is how the platform knows things before they become crises. Not because the data showed it. Because someone said something.

### Human Signal Is the Early Warning System

Data is always a lagging indicator. It shows what happened after it happened. A streaming number drops *after* the audience loses interest. An earnings anomaly appears *after* something went wrong upstream. A Cue goes unanswered *after* the investor community has already lost confidence.

Human conversation is a leading indicator. People feel things before the data registers them.

An artist saying *"I'm really struggling to stay motivated"* is not a data point any API sends. But it is the early signal of a journey that is about to fall behind schedule — which means a cohort timeline at risk, investors who need to be managed, a milestone that won't hit. The platform that hears this in week two can respond in week two. The platform that waits for the data to show it responds in month four.

The conversational layer turns this early signal into actionable intelligence. Not by surveillance. By invitation. The platform creates a relationship where participants naturally share what's happening — because they get something useful back every time they do.

### What the System Already Knows Before the Conversation Starts

When any participant opens a conversation, the system already has their full context loaded:

**For an artist:** current milestone stage, timeline position against the cohort calendar, streaming trajectory this week vs. last week, investment raise status, upcoming unlock triggers, any flagged anomalies in their earnings data, their band agreement terms, every pending action item in their journey.

**For an investor:** full portfolio across all artists and products, earnings to date broken down by source, unrealized upside based on current trajectory, Cues pending their response, any artists in their portfolio with flagged anomalies.

**For a band member:** their individual documented stake in every recording they contributed to, their direct earnings history, any disputes or holds on their account, their profile and tag visibility.

**For a fan:** artists they've pledged on, how those artists are performing, upcoming events, Cues waiting for them.

The conversation begins where the data leaves off. The system doesn't ask who they are or what they're invested in. It already knows. It asks what they're feeling or what they need — because that is the one thing the data cannot tell it.

### The Interface Hierarchy — Conversation First, Everything Else Fallback

The most important design principle for the Voice Layer is what it replaces. The traditional app interface presents the user with a dashboard full of tasks, notifications, and badges and asks them to navigate it. The platform engages the user first and only falls back to colder interface patterns when the conversation hasn't produced what's needed.

**Primary — Conversation:** Proactive, warm, relational, contextual. The platform initiates when it has something to say or needs something. The participant responds how they want, through whatever channel they're in. The platform extracts what it needs from natural language, not from form fields.

**Secondary — Tasks:** Appear on the dashboard when structured data is needed and conversation hasn't collected it. The fallback for participants who go quiet or prefer the cold interface.

**Tertiary — Notifications:** Reserved for genuinely time-sensitive signals that cannot wait for the next natural conversation moment.

**Reinforcement — Gamification:** Badges, streaks, and milestone celebrations that mark what the conversation already produced. The badge is the acknowledgment, not the mechanism.

The cold dashboard — tasks stacked in a list, notifications in a feed, gamification badges counting up — is what the platform looks like if a participant never engages conversationally. It is the safety net. The relationship is the experience.

### MCP-Mediated Feedback — Signal Without a Comment Section

The music industry has never solved the problem of how to collect genuine audience signal without creating a toxic feedback environment. Comment sections reward volume and emotion, not relevance. Ratings are gameable and reductive. Direct messages are unscalable. None of them produce the kind of signal that's actually useful to an artist, an investor, or a platform.

The platform's MCP server solves this by listening to what's already being said — in the conversational interface, in text exchanges, in email replies — and deciding what is worth surfacing and to whom.

Not because someone posted a review. Because the intelligence recognized something meaningful.

**How it works:**

Every conversation on the platform is monitored by the MCP server's signal detection layer. When something significant emerges — a fan expressing a specific reaction to a song, a SteakHolder articulating a concern, a listener describing an emotional experience — the system evaluates it against a set of criteria:

- Is this specific enough to be actionable?
- Does it contain information the recipient couldn't derive from the data alone?
- Is it relevant to a decision the artist or platform is currently facing?
- Would the originator be comfortable knowing this was shared in this way?

If it passes, it is routed — anonymized or attributed, depending on context — to the appropriate recipient as a curated signal item, not a notification.

**What the artist sees:**

Not a comment feed. A weekly signal digest:

> *"Three separate fans, in separate conversations, described 'No Signal' as the song they put on when they're driving alone at night. One described specifically what the bridge does to them — unprompted. Thought you'd want to know what's landing."*

> *"A SteakHolder flagged in conversation that they're finding it hard to explain to friends why they invested. They're not wavering — they believe in it — but they don't have language for it yet. This might be worth addressing in your next community update."*

The artist doesn't get every opinion. They get the signal the system judged worth their attention — curated for relevance, not sorted by volume. No reply-guys. No pile-ons. No ratings. Meaning, not noise.

**What the platform team sees:**

The MCP server also surfaces patterns across the full conversation corpus that are relevant to platform-level decisions — recurrent friction points, widespread confusion about a feature, a sentiment shift in the investor community, an emerging concern in the artist cohort. These are platform signals, not individual participant data, and they inform platform decisions without requiring a formal survey or a public forum.

**The privacy principle:**

The system surfaces the substance of a signal, never the identity of the source unless the source has explicitly agreed to attribution. A fan who tells the platform something meaningful about a song does not expect to be forwarded to the artist. The platform protects that expectation while still ensuring the meaningful thing gets heard.

### No Comment Sections — The Platform Has None

This is a hard platform principle, not a moderation preference.

SoundSteak has no publicly accessible comment sections anywhere on the platform. No reply threads under releases. No public reaction feeds. No ratings. No open forums. This is not a policy that gets revisited when the platform scales — it is structural, permanent, and intentional.

Every major platform that started with community ended up with moderation hell. Comment sections reward volume and outrage, not relevance. They become the place where the signal drowns in the noise and where culture goes to corrode. The platform is architected around that outcome from day one.

**What this does not mean:**

Expression is not silenced — it is redirected. Fans, SteakHolders, and artists have full access to expression through other channels. The platform is not restrictive. It is deliberate. What gets said publicly has been earned — by quality, by relevance, or by community consensus. Not by whoever typed the most or the loudest.

**The expression stack:**

|---|---|---|
| Governance votes | Primary expression — preferences, direction, community opinion | Results visible; individual votes private |
| Conversational interface | Direct engagement with the platform's intelligence | Platform only |
| MCP-surfaced signals | Patterns worth amplifying from private expression | Curated, anonymized, routed to artist or platform |
| Approved public moments | Milestone reactions, notable insights explicitly elevated | Public, requires approval or a Cue |

### Cues as the Primary Expression Vehicle

Cues are not a compliance mechanism. They are the primary way every participant expresses themselves, makes decisions, and takes action on the platform.

Instead of "leave a comment," the platform initiates a conversation: *"What do you think?"* The artist or platform frames the question. The participant answers it in their own words or selects from presented options. No one can go off topic. No one can hijack the narrative. The expression stays inside the rails of what actually matters — and produces clean, actionable signal instead of noise.

**The Cue IS the comment:**

A fan who wants to say "you should play Chicago" doesn't type it into a box where it competes with 400 other opinions. The platform asks them — in a Cue — what city they'd most want to see the artist play. The artist sees "Chicago — 67% of your SteakHolders." One signal. Zero inbox management.

**What expression Cues carry:**

- Tour routing preferences (cities, venue size, timing)
- Artwork direction (multiple options, community selects)
- Collaborator nominations (who should the artist work with next)
- Milestone priorities (what the community wants to see happen next)
- Platform-level decisions that affect the investor community
- Release timing preferences
- Community moments worth preserving publicly

**What action Cues carry:**

- Split confirmations and band agreement signatures
- Identity verification and KYC completion
- AI licensing preference declaration
- Publishing split acknowledgment
- Collaborator credit confirmation
- Milestone approvals
- Investor decisions on artist proposals

**The record it creates:**

Every Cue is permanent and timestamped — the artist can look back at any point in their journey and see what their community expressed at every stage. The community can see how often the artist acted on what they said. This creates accountability in both directions without anyone having to argue about it publicly.

**The equality of the Cue:**

Every participant's response carries equal weight in expression Cues regardless of stake size, follower count, or how loudly they might have typed in a comment section. The fan with $1 and the investor with $10,000 have the same voice when the platform asks what they think. This is a deliberate structural choice.

### Organic Reviews — Extracted, Not Requested

SoundSteak does not ask for reviews. There is no star rating. No "how was your experience?" prompt. No end-of-session feedback form. Asking someone to leave a review is asking them to perform a feeling they may or may not have had — and the output reflects that. The conversion rate is low, the language is stiff, and the result serves the platform more than the artist.

Instead, the platform listens for the feeling when it arrives on its own.

**How it works:**

When a fan is in natural conversation with the platform — talking about an artist, describing what a song did to them, explaining why they pledged — the AI recognizes when something review-worthy is being said. Not keywords. Not a rating. A genuine expression: *"I was Founding SteakHolder #23. I heard this when they had 400 followers. Something about it just landed. I knew."*

At that moment, a Cue Card surfaces:

> *"Do you want to use this as a review for The Hollow North? They would love hearing from you."*

The fan sees their own words quoted back — exactly as they said them. They can approve it as-is, edit it, or decline. If they approve, it becomes a verified, attributed review on the artist's profile. If they decline, the sentiment feeds the anonymous signal layer and nothing is published.

**Why this produces better reviews than any review system:**

The feeling came first. The platform didn't manufacture the moment — it caught one that was already happening. The language is real because it wasn't written for an audience. And the attribution is meaningful: the review carries the fan's verified profile context — Founding SteakHolder status, discovery record, artists backed. A short sentence from someone with that profile is more credible than a paragraph from an anonymous username.

**What artists see:**

Reviews appear on the artist's profile as attributed quotes, each carrying the reviewer's profile credentials. Not a star average. Not a score. A documented human saying something true about the music, verified by the system that knows exactly who they are and what their relationship to that artist has been.

**The privacy principle holds:**

The fan is never forwarded to the artist without consent. The Cue Card is the consent mechanism. Before that moment, the conversation is private. The platform catches the signal — it doesn't surveil and redistribute without asking.

**Verified authenticity — structurally, not by policy:**

Every other review platform takes a text field on faith. SoundSteak can prove the review is real.

The platform has the full conversation log. It knows the review wasn't typed into a submission form — it emerged from a live interaction the fan was already having. It knows the fan's verified identity, their relationship to the artist, and the timestamp of the moment the feeling was expressed. It can confirm the language matches the conversational register of that specific person across their history on the platform.

It can also confirm what the review is not:

- **Not written by the artist.** The conversation originated with the fan's verified profile. The artist has no access to another user's conversation thread.
- **Not AI-generated.** The platform initiated and conducted the conversation. It knows what its own model produced and what the human wrote. A fan's natural language response in a live conversation is distinguishable from generated text by the system that generated the context around it.

This is not a moderation feature. It is a structural property of how the review came to exist. The authenticity is provable because the mechanism that produced it is documented end-to-end.

**The compounding effect:**

A fan with a strong discovery record approving a review is a signal the industry has never had before: verified early believers saying publicly what they thought before the world agreed. That's not a review. That's provenance.

### Regular Mediation — Artist, Vendors, and SteakHolders

Mediation on SoundSteak is not a dispute resolution tool. It is a scheduled platform rhythm.

At defined points in the release cycle, the platform facilitates structured alignment between the three parties who have something at stake in the outcome: the artist creating the work, the vendors delivering the services, and the SteakHolders who funded the release. These check-ins are not optional and not informal — they are part of the contract everyone signed into when the gong rang. The platform holds them.

**Why regular rather than reactive:**

Most professional relationships in music fail not because of malice but because of drift. A studio falls three weeks behind and never says so. A mastering engineer delivers something that doesn't match the brief and nobody escalates it cleanly. Investors feel like they're watching a black box and start filling the silence with anxiety. None of these require a lawsuit — they require a structured moment where everyone is in the same conversation at the same time.

The platform creates that moment on a schedule, before the drift becomes a crisis.

**The three-party mediation structure:**

Every mediation moment involves all three parties in sequence through the Cue system — not a single group call where one party dominates, but a structured async loop:

1. **Vendor Cue** — The platform asks the vendor to report on delivery status, any blockers, and whether the service-for-equity arrangement is being honored as agreed. This is filed first — the other parties see it only after the vendor has submitted.

2. **Artist Cue** — The platform asks the artist to confirm their experience of the service so far, whether milestones are on track from their side, and whether any issues need surfacing. They see the vendor's report before they respond, but they respond independently.

3. **SteakHolder Cue** — SteakHolders receive a synthesized summary of both reports, framed against the release timeline. They respond through a formal governance vote: are they satisfied with progress, do they want to flag a concern, or do they call for a resolution Cue?

The outputs of all three Cues are documented on the platform permanently — timestamped, attributed, visible to all parties. No private side channels. No he-said-she-said later.

**When mediation escalates:**

If any party flags a concern, the platform opens a resolution Cue — a more structured conversation with defined options: extend the timeline, renegotiate terms, bring in a neutral, or trigger the contract's dispute clause. The resolution Cue has a deadline. If it isn't resolved by the deadline, it escalates to a human mediator (initially the platform team; eventually a defined third-party music industry mediator).

The escalation path is documented in the contract before anyone signs. Nobody discovers the rules in the middle of a dispute.

**Cadence:**

The exact schedule is determined by the release timeline, but a baseline structure:

| Point in cycle | Mediation focus |
|---|---|
| Pre-production complete | Vendor delivery confirmed, SteakHolder briefed on timeline |
| Recording complete | Studio and engineer sign-off, artist confirms quality, investors see milestone hit |
| Mix and master complete | Final delivery chain confirmed, equity stakes reaffirmed |
| Release week | All parties aligned on rollout plan, marketing milestones, revenue projections |
| 30 days post-release | First earnings picture, any discrepancies flagged, SteakHolder Q1 update |
| 90 days post-release | Trajectory review, ongoing equity earning confirmed, any renegotiation open window |

**The vendor-as-SteakHolder dynamic:**

When a professional takes equity instead of cash, they become a SteakHolder themselves — which creates a natural alignment of interests but also a new relationship to manage. They are simultaneously a service provider accountable to the artist and an investor accountable to the other SteakHolders. The mediation structure holds both roles cleanly: their vendor Cue covers delivery, their SteakHolder Cue covers investment. The platform keeps the two tracks distinct so neither obligation gets collapsed into the other.

**What SteakHolders actually want:**

SteakHolders don't want to manage the release. They want to know it's being managed. The mediation rhythm gives them exactly that — regular, documented evidence that the process is on track and that they will hear about it if it isn't. This converts investor anxiety (a silent, corrosive force) into investor confidence. Confidence is what drives referrals, secondary sharing, and long-term platform loyalty.

### The Efficient Quotient
The music rights infrastructure is siloed by design and by neglect:

SoundSteak's onboarding forces everything to connect at the **point of creation** — before the music is released, not after the money goes missing. ## Vertical Integration — Owning the Infrastructure

Every dollar that leaves the ecosystem to pay a third-party vendor is a dollar that could stay inside it. The long-term strategy is to progressively own or partner into the infrastructure the platform depends on — removing margin leaks, reducing artist costs, increasing investor returns, and building assets that have value independent of the platform itself.

### The Three Stages

**Stage 1 — Preferred Partnerships (Year 1-2)**
Direct artist business toward specific studios, pressing plants, merch fulfillers, and distributors. Partners get volume, visibility, and ad placement in front of a uniquely engaged music audience. The platform gets negotiated rates, revenue share, and preferential terms for artists. No capital required. Ad inventory is the opening offer in every partnership negotiation — it costs the platform nothing to give and is worth real money to the right partner. Relationships begin immediately — several already exist in the founder's network.

**Stage 2 — Equity Stakes (Year 3-4)**
Once the platform has volume and leverage, take minority equity stakes in the vendors doing the most business with platform artists. Aligns incentives without full acquisition capital. The platform benefits when the vendor grows. The vendor gets a reliable, growing pipeline of funded artists.

**Stage 3 — Ownership (Year 4+)**
Acquire assets outright when capital and opportunity align. Each acquisition removes a margin leak and adds a capability the platform fully controls.

### The Infrastructure Acquisition Roadmap

| Asset | Why It Matters | Stage |
|---|---|---|
| Recording studio partnership | Preferred rates for cohort artists, revenue share on outside bookings | Year 1 — network exists |
| Merch fulfillment partnership | Control quality and margins on artist merch | Year 1 |
| Distribution partnership | Pipeline from recording to streaming | Year 1-2 |
| Vinyl pressing partnership | Preferred rates, priority queue for platform artists | Year 2-3 |
| Music video production partnership | Preferred video capability | Year 2-3 |
| Publishing administration | Collect mechanicals directly, keep the percentage in-house | Year 3+ |
| Recording studio equity stake | Deeper alignment, share in growth | Year 3-4 |
| Vinyl pressing facility acquisition | Own the capacity entirely | Year 4-5 |
| Merch fulfillment ownership | Full margin capture on all artist merch | Year 4-5 |
| Distribution ownership | Full control of the earnings pipeline | Year 5+ |

### Vinyl — A Strategic First Target
Vinyl production is experiencing a genuine renaissance. Demand has outpaced supply for years. Pressing plants are backlogged. Lead times are long. Costs are high. An artist pressing 500 units of their first single is at the back of a long line paying a premium.

A platform that owns pressing capacity has something genuinely scarce:
- Serves its own artists first, at cost — a direct benefit to every cohort
- Presses for outside artists at market rate as an independent revenue stream
- The plant runs full because platform demand feeds it and outside demand covers the rest
- It is a real physical asset with real revenue independent of the platform's success — it does not go to zero if the investment side has a difficult year

### Publishing Administration — The Underrated Target
Most platforms farm publishing administration to Songtrust or similar services that take 15-25% of mechanical royalties for global collection. A platform with enough artists can justify building its own publishing administration arm. At scale that percentage stays inside the ecosystem. This is one of the highest-leverage acquisitions on the roadmap because it compounds across every artist and every song on the platform indefinitely.

### The Compounding Logic
Each asset acquired reduces costs for artists → increases their earnings → increases investor returns → attracts more investors → attracts more artists → justifies acquiring the next asset. The flywheel and the vertical integration strategy reinforce each other. The platform grows stronger at both ends simultaneously.

**The AI Angle — What Sophisticated Investors Need to Hear**

Most music investment opportunities are backward-looking: buying catalogs that already exist, at multiples that already reflect their peak value. SoundSteak is the only model where investors get in before the catalog exists — and own it through an era where the catalog itself is becoming a new kind of asset class.

AI companies are actively acquiring licensed music catalogs for training data, style models, and generative tools. The legal battle over unlicensed use is well underway. What they need — what they'll pay a premium for — is a catalog with clean provenance: documented ownership at every level, registered splits, filed rights, and clear licensing terms set by the artist at the moment of signing.

SoundSteak builds that catalog by default. Every release has:
- A registered LLC with documented ownership percentages
- ISRC, PRO, SoundExchange, and MLC registrations filed before recording begins
- Artist-defined AI licensing terms built into the contract and enforced at the API level
- A metered developer console that AI companies and developers use to access the catalog at agreed rates — per artist, per use case, per the terms the artist already set

This is not a future feature. It is the structure. An investor backing SoundSteak is backing a company that will hold the most legally clean, most clearly documented, most AI-ready catalog of independent music in existence — because clean provenance is baked into the process, not retrofitted after the fact.

The rest of the industry will spend the next decade trying to get their catalogs into this shape. SoundSteak starts there on day one.
